\begin{abstract}
Frontier AI laboratories racing toward Artificial General Intelligence
(AGI) face uncertain timing of transformative demand and must decide how
much irreversible capacity to build and how to split it between
inference (current revenue) and training (future capability). I develop
a real-options model with regime switching, duopoly competition,
endogenous default risk, and diminishing returns calibrated to AI
scaling laws. The model delivers analytical investment triggers for the
single firm and a semi-analytical duopoly preemption equilibrium in
which the leader trains more and invests earlier than the follower. A
``faith-based survival'' mechanism emerges: training raises the expected
post-AGI continuation value, lowering the default boundary---the hope of
AGI keeps the firm alive. An asymmetric dilemma arises: conservative
underinvestment is costlier in expected value, but aggressive
overinvestment carries substantially higher tail default risk.
Calibration to four AI lab archetypes illustrates how heterogeneity in
beliefs about AI timelines drives cross-sectional variation in
investment behavior.
\end{abstract}


\setstretch{1.25}
\section{Introduction}\label{sec-introduction}

The race to build artificial intelligence infrastructure is the defining
capital allocation event of the 2020s. The leading AI laboratories and
their compute partners committed over \$200 billion in 2024 alone, with
combined capital expenditure projected to reach \$660--690 billion in
2026 \citep{palmer2026amazon, alphabet2025q4, microsoft2026q2}. OpenAI's
Stargate joint venture with SoftBank, Oracle, and MGX alone commits up
to \$500 billion in U.S. AI infrastructure through 2029, with an initial
deployment of \$100 billion \citep{openai2025stargate}.\footnote{The
  centrality of compute to AI capability has made it a focal point for
  governance: governments and firms increasingly use compute allocation,
  monitoring, and export controls as levers for AI safety and industrial
  policy; see \citet{sastry2024computing} for a comprehensive treatment.}
These investments are largely irreversible, yet the fundamental
uncertainty driving them, whether and when AI capabilities will reach a
transformative threshold, remains unresolved.

A frontier AI laboratory produces intelligence by combining two forms of
compute. \emph{Training compute} is used to optimize a neural network's
parameters on massive datasets, yielding a model whose quality scales as
a power law of compute invested
\citep{kaplan2020scaling, altman2025observations}. \emph{Inference
compute} runs the trained model to serve predictions and generate
revenue. Both activities consume the same scarce hardware: purpose-built
GPU clusters housed in multi-billion-dollar data centers. This creates
an allocation problem with no close parallel in traditional capital
budgeting.\footnote{In practice, AI infrastructure investment also
  encompasses substantial expenditure on power generation and
  transmission (dedicated natural gas plants, long-term power purchase
  agreements, on-site substations) required to supply the hundreds of
  megawatts that a single training cluster demands. For tractability, I
  model all of these complementary investments as a single capital
  outlay in ``compute capacity,'' with the cost function
  \(I(K) = cK^\gamma\) absorbing the full cost of bringing a unit of
  capacity online, including its power supply.}

These facilities take years to build. Large-scale training clusters
involve substantial configuration, networking, and software investments
that create adjustment costs when reallocating compute between training
and inference, even though the underlying GPU hardware is physically
fungible. Executives frame the risk calculus in starkly asymmetric
terms. Alphabet CEO Sundar Pichai has argued that ``the risk of
underinvesting is dramatically greater than the risk of overinvesting''
\citep{pichai2024risk}, while Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang contends that
agentic AI and reasoning require ``easily 100x more'' compute than
anticipated just one year prior \citep{huang2025scaling}. The prize
these investments target is variously called ``transformative AI'' or
``Artificial General Intelligence'' (AGI): a step change in model
capability sufficient to automate a broad range of cognitive tasks and
generate sustained, qualitatively higher demand for compute
\citep{amodei2024machines}. Executive timeline estimates for this event
range from ``superintelligence in a few thousand days''
\citep{altman2024intelligence}, 2026--2027 \citep{amodei2024machines},
and ``AGI in 2026'' \citep{musk2026agi} to ``not with current
architectures'' \citep{lecun2025autoregressive}. This dispersion in
beliefs is precisely what makes the investment problem so acute: firms
must commit irreversible capital based on profoundly different priors
about the future.

The central question is how a firm should time, size, and allocate an
irreversible capacity investment when the return to that capacity
depends on a regime change whose arrival is uncertain and whose
competitive consequences hinge on how the capacity was used before it
arrived. Existing frameworks illuminate parts of this problem but not
its core difficulty. Real options models
\citep{mcdonald1986value, dixit1994investment} characterize
irreversibility and timing but take the use of installed capacity as
given; R\&D race models \citep{loury1979market, reinganum1982strategic}
capture competitive intensity but treat pre-breakthrough expenditure as
pure cost with no concurrent revenue; structural credit models
\citep{leland1994corporate} handle leverage and default but not
regime-switching growth options with endogenous capacity allocation.
What is missing is the interaction among these forces. A firm's decision
to bet on the future by allocating capacity to training simultaneously
weakens its ability to survive to that future by diverting capacity from
revenue-generating inference. This tension makes the investment problem
fundamentally different from standard capacity choice: the option to
grow and the risk of failure are not merely coexistent but mechanically
linked through the same allocation decision.

This paper develops a model that places this allocation problem at the
center of the investment decision. A firm chooses when to invest, how
much capacity to install, and what fraction to devote to training versus
inference, facing regime-switching demand \citep{guo2005investment},
duopoly preemption \citep{grenadier2002option, huisman2015strategic},
endogenous default \citep{leland1994corporate}, and diminishing returns
calibrated to empirical scaling laws
\citep{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training}. The primary
contribution is the identification and characterization of the
training-survival channel: because the same capacity serves both current
revenue and future capability, the allocation decision links a firm's
growth option value to its default boundary, producing mechanisms
(faith-based survival, asymmetric belief costs, leader-follower training
divergence) that no subset of existing models can generate. The results
fall into three categories.

First, firms choose not only when to invest and how much capacity to
install, but how to split that capacity between model training (which
builds capabilities for the future) and inference serving (which
generates revenue today). In the current (low) regime, revenue depends
on inference capacity through a Tullock contest; in the post-AGI (high)
regime, revenue depends on training quality. This regime-specific
competition creates a genuine strategic trade-off absent from standard
capacity models: a firm that allocates too much to training sacrifices
current revenue and risks default, while a firm that allocates too
little sacrifices its competitive position in the transformative AI
regime. I provide analytical characterization of the optimal training
fraction, investment trigger, and capacity. Duopoly preemption changes
both timing and training incentives: the leader allocates more to
training than the follower because the monopoly phase eliminates the
contest-share cost of training. With no rival, the leader's market share
is insensitive to its training allocation, reducing the marginal cost of
training relative to the duopoly phase.

Second, the interaction of training allocation with leverage and default
risk produces a \emph{faith-based survival} mechanism: training
investment raises the effective revenue coefficient \(A_{\text{eff}}\)
through the expected post-AGI continuation value, lowering the default
boundary. This channel operates only in parameter regions where it
dominates current cash-flow weakness, a condition I characterize in
closed form for the symmetric duopoly.

Third, I introduce \emph{Dario's dilemma}: the cost to a firm of
investing according to a belief about AGI timing that differs from
reality. The asymmetry of this trade-off is captured by Anthropic CEO
Dario Amodei's observation: ``I could buy \$1 trillion of compute that
starts at the end of 2027. If my revenue is not \$1 trillion dollars, if
it's even \$800 billion, there's no force on Earth, there's no hedge on
Earth that could stop me from going bankrupt''
\citep{amodei2026dwarkesh}. If a firm invests as though AGI is less
likely than it truly believes, it invests too late and loses first-mover
advantage; if it invests as though AGI is more likely, it overbuilds and
risks default. The training channel amplifies this asymmetry: an overly
optimistic firm over-allocates to training, sacrificing the inference
revenue it needs to survive. A stylized calibration to four AI lab
archetypes illustrates that the asymmetry is substantial: aggressive
overinvestment carries higher downside risk than conservative
underinvestment, and the observed capital commitments are consistent
with genuinely optimistic beliefs about AI timelines.

The model focuses on duopoly as the core strategic setting. Frontier AI
training competition is highly concentrated: as of 2025, the vast
majority of the world's largest training runs are conducted by a handful
of laboratories, often backed by or partnered with a single cloud
provider (e.g., Anthropic--Amazon, OpenAI--Microsoft) or building
proprietary clusters (e.g., xAI's Colossus). The dominant strategic
margin is not atomistic competition but rivalry among a few labs, each
making multi-billion-dollar capacity commitments. Duopoly is the minimal
model that captures the key forces (strategic preemption, race-like
training allocation incentives, and continuation-value effects under
leverage) while maintaining analytical tractability.\footnote{An \(N>2\)
  extension along the lines of \citet{bouis2009multistage} may be
  feasible but would sacrifice the closed-form characterization that
  makes the duopoly results transparent.}

\subsection{Related literature}\label{sec-literature}

This paper sits at the intersection of real options, strategic
investment, structural credit risk, and the economics of AI. I organize
the review around the four building blocks and then state the marginal
contribution.

\textbf{Real options and capacity choice.} The foundation rests on
\citet{mcdonald1986value} for optimal timing of irreversible investment
and \citet{pindyck1988irreversible} for capacity choice.\footnote{Other
  foundational contributions include \citet{brennan1985evaluating},
  \citet{bernanke1983irreversibility}, \citet{majd1987time},
  \citet{barilan1996investment}, and \citet{abeleberly1996optimal} on
  costly reversibility.} \citet{guo2005investment} model demand with
regime-dependent drift and volatility, showing large investment jumps
upon favorable regime arrival; \citet{decamps2006irreversible} study
irreversible investment with alternative projects under regime
switching, using a two-regime structure with an absorbing state that is
close to the present paper's \(L \to H\) transition. The key difference
is that their framework does not include strategic interaction,
training-inference allocation, or default risk.\footnote{\citet{bloom2009impact}
  develops time-varying volatility shocks; \citet{bloom2007uncertainty}
  provide firm-level validation; \citet{jovanovic2005general} document
  S-curve diffusion for general-purpose technologies.}

\textbf{Strategic investment.} \citet{grenadier2002option} shows that
competition drastically erodes the option value of waiting.\footnote{See
  also \citet{grenadier1996strategic} on development cascades,
  \citet{back2009open} on closed-loop strategies,
  \citet{weeds2002strategic} on R\&D delay, and
  \citet{fudenberg1985preemption} for game-theoretic foundations.}
\citet{huisman2015strategic} is the closest strategic investment paper
in which they study a duopoly with joint timing and capacity choice. I
extend their framework with endogenous default risk, training-inference
allocation, diminishing returns, and regime switching.
\citet{lambrecht2003real} study real options in duopoly with incomplete
information, where firms have private signals about the investment
payoff; their setting is related to the present paper's heterogeneous
beliefs about \(\lambda\), but they do not model the training-inference
allocation or the endogenous default mechanism that links beliefs to
survival. The present paper's emphasis on heterogeneous beliefs about
\(\lambda\) as a driver of cross-sectional variation connects to the
broader literature on disagreement and speculation
\citep{harrison1978speculative, scheinkman2003overconfidence}, though
here disagreement operates through comparative statics in \(\lambda\)
rather than through equilibrium trading under heterogeneous priors.
\citet{grenadier2011real} develop real options signaling games in which
investment timing conveys private information---relevant to the future
direction of dynamic belief updating about \(\lambda\) discussed in
Section~\ref{sec-conclusion}. \citet{bouis2009multistage} provide an
\(N\)-firm sequential entry framework that could extend the duopoly
analysis.

\textbf{R\&D races and arms race dynamics.} The investment dynamics in
this paper also connect to the literature on patent races and R\&D
competition. \citet{loury1979market} and \citet{lee1980market} model
firms choosing R\&D intensity to accelerate a stochastic Poisson
breakthrough, showing that competition leads to socially excessive
duplication of effort. \citet{reinganum1981dynamic} and
\citet{reinganum1982strategic} extend the framework to asymmetric firms,
demonstrating that the frontrunner invests more aggressively---analogous
to the leader's higher training fraction in Proposition 3(ii).
\citet{harris1985patent} and \citet{harris1987racing} model R\&D as a
race with position-dependent investment incentives, producing a
``discouragement effect'' for lagging firms that echoes the follower's
reduced training allocation in the duopoly equilibrium.
\citet{grossman1986optimal} study simultaneous timing and scale choices
in R\&D competition under uncertainty, combining elements of the
capacity and timing decisions modeled here. The key departure from this
literature is the dual role of capacity: in standard R\&D races,
investment during the race is pure cost with no concurrent revenue,
whereas here, installed capacity generates inference revenue pre-AGI and
training quality determines competitive position post-AGI, creating the
allocation trade-off that is absent from existing race models.

\textbf{Structural credit risk and investment-financing interactions.}
The default mechanism builds on \citet{merton1974pricing} and
\citet{leland1994corporate}.\footnote{\citet{blackcox1976valuing} and
  \citet{goldstein2001ebit} extend to first-passage and dynamic
  settings.} The most closely related papers combining investment and
default are \citet{hackbarth2014capital} (real options with endogenous
default and product market competition) and \citet{kumar2018optimal}
(joint leverage and irreversible capacity choice, showing they are
substitutes).\footnote{\citet{sundaresan2015dynamic} model growth
  options with default risk; \citet{bolton2011unified} and
  \citet{bolton2019investment} model financial constraints;
  \citet{morellec2004can} studies endogenous investment and capital
  structure; \citet{strebulaev2007tests} develops dynamic capital
  structure with infrequent adjustment; \citet{hennessy2004tobin},
  \citet{mauer1994interactions}, \citet{childs2005interactions}, and
  \citet{miao2005optimal} further develop financing-investment
  interactions.} The present model integrates endogenous default into
the strategic capacity-choice game, building on
\citet{hackbarth2012corporate} for the investment-financing framework.
The Leland default framework is most natural for public-debt issuers;
for the VC-backed archetypes in the calibration, ``default'' should be
interpreted broadly as financial distress (failed funding round,
liquidity crisis), consistent with \citet{gornall2020squaring} on the
gap between VC valuations and the underlying economics of startup
financing.

\textbf{AI economics and scaling laws.} AI training exhibits power-law
scaling \citep{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training}, motivating the
concave production function; \citet{jones1995rdbased} and
\citet{bloom2020ideas} study diminishing returns in idea production.
\citet{agrawal2019economics} provide a comprehensive framework for AI as
a prediction technology, connecting inference demand to economic value
creation. On the training-inference allocation,
\citet{akcigit2018growth} study exploitation versus exploration
R\&D---the closest analog, but in a multi-product firm rather than a
compute allocation setting.\footnote{\citet{berk1999optimal} and
  \citet{carlson2004corporate} develop growth option decompositions that
  motivate the value decomposition in Section~\ref{sec-valuation}.
  \citet{katz1986technology} and \citet{farrell1986installed} study
  technology adoption with network externalities and installed-base
  inertia. \citet{babina2024artificial} and
  \citet{eisfeldt2024generative} provide empirical context on AI
  investment and valuations; these papers could in principle discipline
  certain calibration inputs (e.g., AI firm betas, investment
  intensities), though the rapidly evolving nature of the sector limits
  the stability of such estimates.} On the macroeconomics of AI,
\citet{acemoglu2024simple}, \citet{korinek2024scenarios}, and
\citet{jones2024agi} analyze growth implications of transformative AI in
general equilibrium. The present paper is partial equilibrium---it takes
the demand process and discount rate as given---and therefore
complements these general equilibrium analyses by providing a
micro-founded model of the firm-level investment and allocation
decisions that aggregate into the industry dynamics these papers
study.\footnote{\citet{dellaseta2012optimal} combine real options with
  learning curves; \citet{novymarx2007operating} shows that operating
  leverage amplifies systematic risk, relevant to the operating cost
  parameter \(\delta\).}

\textbf{Marginal contribution.} The mechanism that existing models
cannot generate is the \emph{training-inference allocation under
regime-specific competition with endogenous default}: the firm's choice
of how to split capacity between current revenue generation and future
capability building, where (a) the relevant competitive variable
switches across regimes (inference capacity today, training quality
tomorrow), (b) the allocation determines competitive position
differently across regimes, and (c) the allocation affects firm survival
through the continuation-value channel in the default boundary. This
mechanism produces two novel results: a \emph{faith-based survival}
condition (Equation~\ref{eq-phi-underbar}) in which training investment
lowers the default boundary by raising the expected post-AGI
continuation value, and an asymmetric \emph{Dario's dilemma} in which
the training channel amplifies the cost of overinvestment relative to
underinvestment. Neither result can be obtained from existing real
options models (which lack the training-inference margin), existing
strategic investment models (which lack endogenous default), or existing
structural credit models (which lack the regime-switching growth option
with capacity allocation).

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec-model} presents the
model environment with training-inference allocation, regime switching,
the single-firm benchmark, and the duopoly equilibrium with default
risk; the analytical and semi-analytical results are contained in this
section. Section~\ref{sec-calibration} describes the stylized
calibration to frontier AI lab archetypes and presents baseline
numerical results. Section~\ref{sec-valuation} develops quantitative
implications: value decomposition, credit risk, Dario's dilemma, and
equity valuation sensitivity. Section~\ref{sec-discussion} considers
policy implications, testable predictions, and limitations.
Section~\ref{sec-conclusion} concludes.

\section{The Model}\label{sec-model}

This section develops the model in three stages: the demand environment
and technology (including the training-inference allocation), the
single-firm benchmark, and the duopoly with default risk.

\subsection{Environment}\label{environment}

Time is continuous. A stochastic demand process \(X_t\) governs the
revenue available to frontier AI labs. Demand follows a geometric
Brownian motion (GBM) with regime-dependent drift and common volatility:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-gbm}{
dX_t = \mu_s X_t \, dt + \sigma X_t \, dW_t, \quad s \in \{L, H\},
}\end{equation} where \(W_t\) is a standard Brownian motion, \(s\)
indexes the demand regime, and \(\sigma > 0\) is the common volatility
parameter.

The economy starts in the low-demand regime \(L\), representing the
current state where AI capabilities generate moderate but growing demand
for compute. A regime switch to the high-demand state \(H\) occurs as a
Poisson event with exogenous arrival rate \(\lambda > 0\), representing
a breakthrough in AI capabilities---a step change in model performance,
emergent capabilities, or widespread commercial adoption---that
permanently shifts demand to a higher trajectory.\footnote{The
  dispersion in executive timeline estimates maps directly to
  heterogeneity in \(\lambda\). \citet{amodei2024machines} predicts AI
  ``smarter than a Nobel Prize winner'' as early as 2026, implying
  \(\lambda \approx 0.30\)--\(0.50\); \citet{hassabis2025breakthroughs}
  gives roughly 50\% probability by the end of the decade
  (\(\lambda \approx 0.10\)--\(0.15\)); and \citet{nadella2025formula}
  proposes an economic test---developed-world GDP growth of 10\%---that
  implicitly pushes the threshold further out. These differences in
  \(\lambda\) drive the heterogeneity in investment behavior analyzed in
  Section~\ref{sec-valuation}.}

The high regime \(H\) is absorbing: once the economy transitions, it
remains in \(H\) permanently. This asymmetric regime structure captures
the \emph{irreversibility of AI progress}: capabilities, once
demonstrated, do not disappear.

I assume \(\mu_H > \mu_L\) (higher growth in the high regime). For the
model to have well-defined present values, I require
\(r > \mu_H > \mu_L\), where \(r\) is the discount rate and \(\mu_s\) is
the risk-adjusted growth rate.\footnote{Following \citet[Chapter
  4]{dixit1994investment}, all valuation is conducted under a single
  \emph{risk-adjusted} (certainty-equivalent) framework. The discount
  rate \(r\) is the firm's weighted average cost of capital (WACC),
  which embeds the equity risk premium and the cost of debt. The growth
  rates \(\mu_L\) and \(\mu_H\) are the physical-measure expected growth
  rates of demand \emph{minus} an appropriate risk premium, so that
  discounting the expected cash flow at rate \(r\) yields the correct
  present value. The volatility \(\sigma\) is the same under the
  physical and risk-adjusted measures (by Girsanov's theorem).
  Equivalently, one can interpret the model as working under a
  risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\) with drift
  \(\mu_s^{\mathbb{Q}} = \mu_s\) (already risk-adjusted) and discount
  rate \(r\) (the WACC), avoiding any double-counting of risk premia.
  This reduced-form approach avoids specifying a complete asset pricing
  model while remaining consistent with no-arbitrage valuation. All
  parameters (\(r\), \(\mu_s\), \(\sigma\)) are therefore risk-adjusted
  throughout the paper.}

\subsection{Technology}\label{technology}

A firm invests an irreversible lump sum \(I(K) = cK^\gamma\) to install
capacity \(K > 0\), where \(\gamma > 1\) captures convex investment
costs: power constraints, GPU supply bottlenecks, data center site
preparation, and construction complexity all contribute to increasing
marginal costs of capacity procurement. This specification nests the
standard linear cost (\(\gamma = 1\), constant returns). The distinct
empirical regularity that AI scaling laws exhibit diminishing returns to
compute \citep{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training}---doubling
compute does not double capability---is captured separately by the
revenue elasticity \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) in the contest functions below.
Two simplifying assumptions should be flagged at the outset: capacity is
fixed once installed (no incremental expansion or mothballing), and the
investment decision is a single irreversible commitment rather than a
sequence of staged options. In practice, firms expand incrementally and
can repurpose compute across workloads; these extensions are discussed
in Section~\ref{sec-discussion}.

\subsubsection{Training-inference
allocation}\label{training-inference-allocation}

A frontier AI laboratory produces intelligence by combining two forms of
compute. \emph{Training compute} optimizes a neural network's parameters
on massive datasets, yielding a model whose quality scales as a power
law of compute invested. \emph{Inference compute} runs the trained model
to serve predictions and generate revenue. Both activities consume the
same scarce hardware---the GPU clusters described in
Section~\ref{sec-introduction} and their associated power
production---creating an allocation problem with no close parallel in
traditional capital budgeting.

A firm with total installed capacity \(K\) allocates fraction
\(\phi \in (0, 1)\) to training and \(1 - \phi\) to inference, such that
training compute is \(\phi K\) and inference compute is \((1-\phi) K\).
Revenue depends on the demand regime:

\begin{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{Low regime} (\(L\), pre-AGI): revenue derives from
  \emph{inference}---serving current demand for AI predictions and
  services. Firm \(i\)'s flow revenue is:
  \begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-revenue-L}{
  \pi_i^L(X) = X \cdot [(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha,
  }\end{equation} where \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) captures diminishing
  returns to inference capacity.
\item
  \textbf{High regime} (\(H\), post-AGI): revenue derives from
  \emph{training quality}---the capability of the firm's models
  determines its competitive position in a world of sustained high
  demand. Firm \(i\)'s flow revenue is:
  \begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-revenue-H}{
  \pi_i^H(X) = X \cdot (\phi_i K_i)^\alpha.
  }\end{equation}
\end{itemize}

This regime-dependent revenue structure captures a fundamental asymmetry
in AI infrastructure: today, inference capacity determines revenue (you
must serve demand), but in a world of transformative AI, training
quality determines competitive position (the best models capture the
market). The training fraction \(\phi\) is chosen at investment time and
fixed thereafter.\footnote{Dynamic reallocation of \(\phi\)
  post-investment would require modeling \(\phi\) as a continuous state
  variable in the HJB equation, substantially increasing computational
  complexity. The static choice is already rich enough to generate the
  key economic trade-offs. I note this as a limitation in
  Section~\ref{sec-discussion}.}

Installed capacity incurs a continuous operating cost at rate
\(\delta \geq 0\) per unit, representing power, cooling, maintenance,
and personnel expenses.\footnote{This is distinct from \emph{economic
  depreciation} (physical decay or technological obsolescence of GPU
  hardware). In the model, capacity \(K\) remains constant once
  installed; \(\delta K\) is a perpetual flow cost, not a decay rate.
  The Section~\ref{sec-calibration} discusses the relationship between
  \(\delta\) and accounting depreciation in detail. Nvidia has released
  new GPU architectures on roughly 18--24 month cycles (A100 in 2020,
  H100 in 2022, B200 in 2024), and algorithmic breakthroughs can
  accelerate obsolescence further: DeepSeek R1 achieved frontier
  reasoning performance using older-generation H800 GPUs at a fraction
  of the expected cost \citep{deepseek2025r1}, demonstrating that
  efficiency gains can devalue installed infrastructure independently of
  hardware cycles.}

Table~\ref{tbl-parameters} and Table~\ref{tbl-notation} in the Appendix
summarize all model parameters and key notation; the calibration
rationale for each value is discussed in Section~\ref{sec-calibration}.

\subsection{Single-Firm Benchmark}\label{sec-single-firm}

I begin with a single firm holding an option to invest. The firm chooses
the demand trigger \(X^*\) at which to invest, the capacity \(K^*\) to
install, and the training fraction \(\phi^*\), maximizing the value of
its investment option.

\subsubsection{Installed value}\label{installed-value}

In the absorbing high regime, the value of installed capacity depends on
training quality:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-installed-value-H}{
V_H(X, K, \phi) = \frac{X (\phi K)^\alpha}{r - \mu_H} - \frac{\delta K}{r} = A_H \cdot X \cdot (\phi K)^\alpha - \frac{\delta K}{r},
}\end{equation} where \(A_H = 1/(r - \mu_H)\). Revenue depends on
training compute \(\phi K\): a firm that allocated all capacity to
inference (\(\phi = 0\)) generates no H-regime revenue, while a firm
that invested heavily in training captures more value in the high-demand
world.

In the low regime, the value of installed capacity combines
\emph{current inference revenue} and the \emph{expected continuation
value} from a potential regime switch to \(H\):
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-installed-value-L}{
V_L(X, K, \phi) = A_{\text{eff}}(\phi, K) \cdot X - \frac{\delta K}{r},
}\end{equation} where the \emph{effective revenue coefficient} is:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-a-eff}{
A_{\text{eff}}(\phi, K) = \frac{[(1-\phi)K]^\alpha}{r - \mu_L + \lambda} + \frac{\lambda}{r - \mu_L + \lambda} \cdot \frac{(\phi K)^\alpha}{r - \mu_H}.
}\end{equation}

The first term capitalizes the L-regime inference revenue stream at the
effective discount rate \(r - \mu_L + \lambda\), which accounts for both
the time value of money and the expected transition out of regime \(L\).
The second term is the present value of the H-regime training revenue
that the firm will earn after the regime switch, weighted by the
probability-rate \(\lambda/(r - \mu_L + \lambda)\) of transitioning
before the discount factor drives the value to zero.

\textbf{Remark} (Nesting). \emph{When \(\phi = 1\) (all training),
\(A_{\text{eff}}\) reduces to
\(\lambda A_H (\phi K)^\alpha / (r - \mu_L + \lambda)\)---value derives
entirely from the regime switch. When \(\phi = 0\) (all inference),
\(A_{\text{eff}}\) reduces to \(K^\alpha / (r - \mu_L + \lambda)\)---the
standard L-regime present value with no H-regime upside. When
\(\lambda = 0\) (no regime switching),
\(A_{\text{eff}} = [(1-\phi)K]^\alpha / (r - \mu_L)\)---only inference
revenue matters.}

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Option value in regime
\(H\)}{Option value in regime H}}\label{option-value-in-regime-h}

In the absorbing high regime, the option to invest at trigger \(X_H^*\)
with capacity \(K_H^*\) and training fraction \(\phi_H^*\) has the
standard real options form \citep{dixit1994investment}:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-option-H}{
F_H(X) = \left(V_H(X_H^*, K_H^*, \phi_H^*) - I(K_H^*)\right) \left(\frac{X}{X_H^*}\right)^{\beta_H}, \quad X < X_H^*,
}\end{equation} where \(\beta_H > 1\) is the positive root of the
H-regime characteristic equation:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-beta-H}{
\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \beta(\beta - 1) + \mu_H \beta - r = 0.
}\end{equation}

The optimal trigger satisfies the smooth-pasting condition:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-trigger-H}{
X_H^* = \frac{\beta_H}{\beta_H - 1} \cdot \frac{(r - \mu_H)}{(\phi K)^{\alpha}} \left(c K^\gamma + \frac{\delta K}{r}\right),
}\end{equation} which shows the standard result that the trigger exceeds
the Marshallian (NPV = 0) level by the factor
\(\beta_H / (\beta_H - 1) > 1\)---the \emph{option premium} that
reflects the value of waiting.

Figure~\ref{fig-option-value} depicts the option value \(F_H(X)\)
alongside the net present value of immediate investment for the baseline
calibration. The option value lies everywhere above the NPV curve, with
the gap---the shaded ``value of waiting'' region---largest at low demand
levels. At \(X = X_H^*\), the two curves meet tangentially (smooth
pasting), and for \(X > X_H^*\) the firm invests immediately so the
option value equals the NPV. The figure illustrates the core real
options result: for \(X < X_H^*\), the firm strictly prefers to wait,
and the magnitude of the value of waiting is substantial---exceeding the
NPV by a factor of two or more at demand levels well below the trigger.

\textbf{Assumption 1} (Parameter admissibility). \emph{The following
conditions are maintained throughout:}

\emph{(A1) \(r > \mu_H > \mu_L \geq 0\), \(\sigma > 0\),
\(\alpha \in (0,1)\), \(\gamma > 1\), \(c > 0\), \(\delta \geq 0\),
\(\lambda > 0\).}

\emph{(A2) The option premium condition
\(1/\gamma < (\beta_H - 1)/(\alpha \beta_H) < 1\) holds, ensuring an
interior capacity solution in regime \(H\).}

\emph{(A3) The L-regime insufficiency condition
\((1 - 1/\beta_L^+)/\alpha \geq 1\) holds, where \(\beta_L^+\) is the
positive root of the L-regime characteristic equation, ensuring that the
simplified option value \(F_L = C \cdot X^{\beta_H}\) applies. This
means that L-regime revenue alone would not justify investment; the firm
may still invest while in regime \(L\), but only because the combined
effective revenue coefficient \(A_{\text{eff}}\)---which includes the
H-regime prospect---makes the investment worthwhile.}

\emph{(A4) \(A_{\text{eff}}(\phi, K)\) is twice continuously
differentiable in \(\phi \in (0,1)\) for fixed \(K > 0\).}

\emph{All four conditions are satisfied at the baseline calibration
(\(\beta_L^+ \approx 3.01\), \((1 - 1/\beta_L^+)/\alpha \approx 1.67\))
and verified numerically at all parameter values used in the paper.
Appendix B provides the full boundary analysis, including sensitivity of
(A2) and (A3) to archetype-specific WACCs and the treatment when (A3)
fails.}

\textbf{Proposition 1} (Optimal capacity and training fraction;
single-firm benchmark). \emph{The firm jointly chooses capacity \(K^*\)
and training fraction \(\phi^*\) to maximize the option value. The
optimal capacity is:} \[
K^* = \left[\frac{\delta\,(\alpha \beta_H - \beta_H + 1)}{r\,c\,(\gamma(\beta_H - 1) - \alpha \beta_H)}\right]^{\frac{1}{\gamma - 1}},
\] \emph{provided \(1/\gamma < (\beta_H - 1)/(\alpha \beta_H) < 1\).
Note that \(K^*\) is independent of \(\phi\): the effective revenue
coefficient takes the form \(A_{\text{eff}} = g(\phi) \cdot K^\alpha\)
(both in the pure H-regime and under regime switching), so the
\(\phi\)-dependent factor cancels in the first-order condition for \(K\)
(see Appendix A, Step 4). The optimal \(\phi^*\) is then determined
separately by maximizing \(A_{\text{eff}}\) over \(\phi\), and the joint
optimum \((K^*, \phi^*)\) yields the investment trigger via smooth
pasting.}

\emph{When the firm operates in the L-regime with the possibility of a
regime switch and the simplified option value
\(F_L = C \cdot X^{\beta_H}\) applies (Assumption 1, condition A3), it
jointly optimizes \((K^*, \phi^*)\) to maximize the option value
\(F(X_0) \propto A_{\text{eff}}(\phi, K)^{\beta_H} / [\delta K/r + cK^\gamma]^{\beta_H - 1}\),
where \(A_{\text{eff}}\) is defined in Equation~\ref{eq-a-eff}. The
optimal training fraction \(\phi^*\) is interior (strictly between 0 and
1) and:}

\emph{(i) \(\phi^*\) is increasing in \(\lambda\): more optimistic
beliefs about the arrival of transformative AI shift allocation toward
training.}

\emph{(ii) \(\phi^*\) is increasing in the H-regime growth rate
\(\mu_H\): a higher post-AGI growth rate increases the relative value of
training, shifting the optimal allocation toward training. Note that
\(\phi^*\) is independent of \(\mu_L\): L-regime drift affects both
inference revenue and the regime-switching discount symmetrically
through the common factor \(1/(r - \mu_L + \lambda)\), leaving the ratio
\(w_H/w_L = \lambda / (r - \mu_H)\) unchanged.}

\emph{Proof.} The joint optimization over \((K, \phi)\) is in Appendix
A. Interiority of \(\phi^*\) follows from the Inada conditions:
\(\partial A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \phi \to +\infty\) as
\(\phi \to 0^+\) and
\(\partial A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \phi \to -\infty\) as
\(\phi \to 1^-\) (since \(\alpha \in (0,1)\)). Uniqueness follows from
strict concavity of \(A_{\text{eff}}\) in \(\phi\) (see Appendix A, Step
5, for the second-derivative argument). The comparative statics follow
from implicit differentiation of the first-order condition; see Appendix
A, Steps 5--6. \(\square\)

In the duopoly (Section~\ref{sec-duopoly}), the contest shares \(s_i^L\)
and \(s_i^H\) depend on own capacity \(K_i\), so the exact separability
\(A_{\text{eff}} = g(\phi) \cdot K^\alpha\) that underlies the
closed-form \(K^*\) does not hold. The follower's optimal capacity is
determined jointly with \(\phi_F\) by numerical optimization. However,
the leader's optimal capacity is numerically identical to the
monopolist's (Proposition 1) across all parameterizations tested---a
regularity attributable to the dominance of the monopoly-phase FOC,
where \(s_L = 1\) and the Proposition 1 separability holds exactly.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Option value in regime
\(L\)}{Option value in regime L}}\label{option-value-in-regime-l}

In the low regime, the firm's investment option reflects both the
possibility of investing in regime \(L\) and the potential for a regime
switch to \(H\). The option value satisfies the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman
equation incorporating the regime-switching term:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-hjb-L}{
\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 X^2 F_L'' + \mu_L X F_L' + \lambda\bigl[F_H(X) - F_L(X)\bigr] - r\, F_L = 0,
}\end{equation} where \(F_H(X) = B_H X^{\beta_H}\) is the known H-regime
\emph{option} value (Equation~\ref{eq-option-H})---the value of the
investment option conditional on being in regime \(H\) without having
invested yet, not the installed value \(V_H\). Substituting and
rearranging, this becomes a second-order Euler ODE with non-homogeneous
term \(\lambda B_H X^{\beta_H}\): \[
\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 X^2 F_L'' + \mu_L X F_L' - (r + \lambda)\, F_L + \lambda B_H X^{\beta_H} = 0.
\]

The general solution has two components. The \emph{homogeneous} solution
has characteristic equation
\(\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \beta(\beta - 1) + \mu_L \beta - (r + \lambda) = 0\),
with positive root \(\beta_L^+\) that depends on \((\sigma, \mu_L)\) and
the effective discount rate \(r + \lambda\). The \emph{particular}
solution, of the form \(C \cdot X^{\beta_H}\), arises from the
regime-switching term, with coefficient:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-particular-C}{
C = \frac{-\lambda\, B_H}{Q_L(\beta_H)}, \quad Q_L(\beta) \equiv \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \beta(\beta - 1) + \mu_L \beta - (r + \lambda).
}\end{equation} Applying the boundary condition \(F_L(0) = 0\) (which
eliminates the negative root) yields:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-option-L-general}{
F_L(X) = A_1 X^{\beta_L^+} + C \cdot X^{\beta_H}, \quad X < X^*.
}\end{equation}

\textbf{When does the simplified form \(F_L = C \cdot X^{\beta_H}\)
apply?} The particular-solution coefficient \(C\) is fully determined by
the ODE's forcing structure (Equation~\ref{eq-particular-C}). The
homogeneous coefficient \(A_1\), by contrast, would be determined by
value-matching and smooth-pasting at a standalone L-regime investment
trigger. When no such trigger exists---specifically, when the option
premium ratio \((1 - 1/\beta_L^+)/\alpha \geq 1\), so that L-regime
revenue by itself is insufficient to justify investment---\(A_1 = 0\)
because there is no L-regime exercise boundary to pin it down. The
remaining boundary conditions (value-matching and smooth-pasting at the
investment trigger driven by the combined \(A_{\text{eff}}\)) then
determine the trigger \(X^*\), with \(C\) already known from the ODE.
This is not an approximation but an exact consequence of Assumption
(A3): the homogeneous term \(A_1 X^{\beta_L^+}\) represents the
autonomous L-regime investment option, and under (A3) this option is
never exercised because L-regime revenue alone is insufficient to
justify irreversible investment at any finite trigger. An option that is
never exercised has zero value, so \(A_1 = 0\) exactly. The formal
argument, including the consistency check that the two boundary
conditions at \(X^*\) are not overdetermined when \(C\) is
predetermined, is given in Appendix A (Step 5b of the Proof of
Proposition 1). For the baseline calibration, the option premium ratio
exceeds unity (\(\Phi_L \approx 1.67\); Assumption 1, condition A3), so
this simplification is valid and the option value derives entirely from
the expected regime switch. Solving the full two-term problem at
baseline confirms that the \(A_1 X^{\beta_L^+}\) term contributes less
than 0.1\% of \(F_L\) at the optimum.

When the simplified form applies, the investment trigger for the joint
\((K^*, \phi^*)\) optimization satisfies:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-trigger-phi}{
X^* = \frac{\beta_H}{\beta_H - 1} \cdot \frac{\delta K^*/r + c(K^*)^\gamma}{A_{\text{eff}}(\phi^*, K^*)},
}\end{equation} which generalizes Equation~\ref{eq-trigger-H} by
replacing the H-regime revenue coefficient with the effective
coefficient \(A_{\text{eff}}\) that combines both regimes. Note that
Equation~\ref{eq-trigger-phi} is an implicit equation:
\(A_{\text{eff}}(\phi^*, K^*)\) itself depends on the optimal policy, so
the trigger is determined jointly with \((K^*, \phi^*)\) through the
system of first-order conditions (Appendix A). The option premium
\(\beta_H / (\beta_H - 1) > 1\) still applies, reflecting the value of
waiting, but the relevant revenue metric now accounts for both L-regime
inference earnings and the expected H-regime training prize.

\textbf{Remark 1} (Role of \(\lambda\)). \emph{The arrival rate
\(\lambda\) has two effects on investment timing and allocation. First,
higher \(\lambda\) increases the weight on the H-regime term in
\(A_{\text{eff}}\) (Equation~\ref{eq-a-eff}), raising the effective
revenue coefficient and lowering the investment trigger---the firm
invests sooner because the expected payoff from the regime switch is
higher. Second, higher \(\lambda\) shifts the optimal training fraction
\(\phi^*\) upward, because the marginal value of training compute (which
determines H-regime revenue) increases relative to the marginal value of
inference compute (which determines L-regime revenue). Higher
\(\lambda\) raises the option value monotonically and encourages earlier
and more training-intensive investment (\(K^*\) itself is independent of
\(\lambda\) by Proposition 1, but the duopoly capacity can differ
through the contest function)---consistent with the observation that AI
labs with more optimistic timeline beliefs invest more aggressively and
allocate more compute to training.}

\(F_L(X)\) increases concavely in \(\lambda\): firms with higher

expect the high-demand regime sooner. The dashed line shows the

Panel (b) plots the particular-solution coefficient \(C\), which
captures the additional value from regime switching; it increases
monotonically in \(\lambda\), confirming that the mechanism operates
through the continuation value channel.

\subsection{Duopoly with Default Risk}\label{sec-duopoly}

I now introduce strategic interaction and leverage. Two symmetric firms,
\(i \in \{1, 2\}\), each hold options to invest in capacity and choose
their training-inference allocation.

\subsubsection{Regime-specific
competition}\label{regime-specific-competition}

Revenue in each regime is governed by a Tullock contest success function
\citep{tullock1980efficient, skaperdas1996contest} over the
regime-relevant capacity measure. The Tullock specification captures the
essential feature of AI compute competition: firms' revenues depend on
\emph{relative} capacity, not just absolute capacity, because customers
(enterprises, developers) allocate workloads to providers based on
relative performance and availability.\footnote{A Cournot
  specification---in which firms choose quantities and price clears a
  downward-sloping demand curve---would be an alternative. The main
  qualitative results (interior \(\phi^*\), faith-based survival,
  preemption) are preserved under Cournot (see Appendix E), but the
  Tullock form is more tractable for the training-inference allocation
  problem because it cleanly separates the capacity and share effects.}
In the L-regime, firms compete over \emph{inference capacity}:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-contest-L}{
\pi_i^L(X, \mathbf{K}, \boldsymbol{\phi}) = X \cdot \frac{[(1-\phi_i)K_i]^{2\alpha}}{[(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha + [(1-\phi_j)K_j]^\alpha},
}\end{equation} which I write equivalently as
\(\pi_i^L = X \cdot [(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha \cdot s_i^L\), where
\(s_i^L = [(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha / \{[(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha + [(1-\phi_j)K_j]^\alpha\}\)
is the L-regime contest share. In the H-regime, firms compete over
\emph{training quality}:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-contest-H}{
\pi_i^H(X, \mathbf{K}, \boldsymbol{\phi}) = X \cdot \frac{(\phi_i K_i)^{2\alpha}}{(\phi_i K_i)^\alpha + (\phi_j K_j)^\alpha},
}\end{equation} with H-regime contest share
\(s_i^H = (\phi_i K_i)^\alpha / \{(\phi_i K_i)^\alpha + (\phi_j K_j)^\alpha\}\).

This regime-specific contest structure formalizes the arms-race logic
that executives describe: in the current market, what matters is the
capacity to \emph{serve} demand (inference), while in a post-AGI world,
what matters is the capability of your \emph{models} (training). When
\(K_j = 0\), both contest shares reduce to \(s_i = 1\) and revenue
simplifies to the single-firm case (Equation~\ref{eq-revenue-L},
Equation~\ref{eq-revenue-H}). The parameter \(\alpha\) controls the
sensitivity of market share to the relevant capacity measure: as
\(\alpha \to 0\), shares approach 50-50 regardless of capacity; as
\(\alpha \to \infty\), the larger firm captures all demand. Three
properties of the Tullock specification under asymmetry merit
discussion. First, under symmetric capacities (\(K_i = K_j\),
\(\phi_i = \phi_j\)), each firm's contest share is \(s_i = 1/2\) and
total industry revenue equals \(X \cdot K^\alpha\), the standard
single-firm benchmark. Second, under \emph{asymmetric} capacities, the
share function is convex in relative capacity: the larger firm's share
exceeds its capacity ratio, producing a ``winner-take-more'' dynamic
consistent with AI markets where the leading model captures a
disproportionate share of API revenue and enterprise contracts. Total
industry revenue
\(\sum_i \pi_i = X \cdot (y_1^2 + y_2^2) / (y_1 + y_2)\), where
\(y_i = [(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha\), can exceed the symmetric benchmark
\(X \cdot (y_1 + y_2)/2\) under asymmetry, because the quadratic mean
exceeds the arithmetic mean whenever \(y_1 \neq y_2\) (equivalently,
\(y_1^2 + y_2^2 \geq (y_1 + y_2)^2/2\) with strict inequality under
asymmetry). Third, with \(\alpha < 1\) the contest is ``regular'' in the
sense of \citet{skaperdas1996contest}: equilibrium is unique, there is
no over-dissipation of rents, and comparative statics are
well-behaved---larger capacity always increases the contest share, but
with diminishing marginal returns. The revenue-inflating property of
asymmetry means that the leader's gain from capacity dominance partly
reflects pie expansion, not just share theft; this could amplify
preemption incentives and leader-follower value gaps beyond what a
fixed-pie specification would deliver. A Cournot specification would
avoid this feature at the cost of additional complexity. Appendix E
verifies that all main qualitative results---interior \(\phi^*\),
faith-based survival, preemption, and the Dario's dilemma
asymmetry---are preserved under Cournot competition, confirming that the
investment and allocation incentives are driven by the underlying
economics rather than the specific contest functional form or the
revenue-expansion property. The training fraction \(\phi\) introduces a
new strategic dimension: a firm's choice of \(\phi\) affects its
competitive position \emph{differently} in each regime, creating a
trade-off between L-regime and H-regime market share.

\subsubsection{Installed value in the
duopoly}\label{installed-value-in-the-duopoly}

The installed value for firm \(i\) in the L-regime combines inference
revenue and the expected H-regime continuation:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-duopoly-value-L}{
V_i^L(X) = A_{\text{eff},i} \cdot X - \frac{\delta K_i}{r},
}\end{equation} where the effective revenue coefficient for firm \(i\)
is: \begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-a-eff-duopoly}{
A_{\text{eff},i} = \frac{[(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha \cdot s_i^L}{r - \mu_L + \lambda} + \frac{\lambda}{r - \mu_L + \lambda} \cdot \frac{(\phi_i K_i)^\alpha \cdot s_i^H}{r - \mu_H}.
}\end{equation}

In the absorbing H-regime: \[
V_i^H(X) = A_H \cdot X \cdot (\phi_i K_i)^\alpha \cdot s_i^H - \frac{\delta K_i}{r}.
\]

The effective revenue coefficient \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) embodies the
central economic trade-off: the first term captures the value of
inference in the current regime (which depends on \((1-\phi_i)K_i\) and
the L-regime contest share), while the second captures the expected
value of training quality in the future regime (which depends on
\(\phi_i K_i\) and the H-regime contest share, weighted by the arrival
rate \(\lambda\)). When \(K_j = 0\) (no rival), the contest shares
reduce to \(s_i^L = s_i^H = 1\) and Equation~\ref{eq-a-eff-duopoly}
reduces to the single-firm \(A_{\text{eff}}\) in
Equation~\ref{eq-a-eff}.

\subsubsection{Capital structure}\label{capital-structure}

Each firm finances investment with a mix of equity and debt. I
parameterize capital structure by the leverage ratio
\(\ell = D_0 / I(K)\), where \(D_0\) is the face value of debt at
issuance. Debt pays a continuous coupon
\(c_D = c_d \cdot \ell \cdot I(K)\) where \(c_d\) is the coupon rate and
\(c_D\) denotes the total coupon payment (used throughout). Following
\citet{leland1994corporate}, equity holders choose to default
endogenously when the equity value falls to zero.

\subsubsection{Default boundary}\label{default-boundary}

Equity holders default endogenously when it is optimal to stop servicing
debt. Following \citet{leland1994corporate} and
\citet{leland1996optimal}, the optimal default boundary is obtained by
smooth pasting. For a levered firm with capacity \(K_i\), training
fraction \(\phi_i\), facing a rival with \((K_j, \phi_j)\):
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-default-boundary}{
X_D = \frac{\beta_s^-}{\beta_s^- - 1} \cdot \frac{c_D / r + \delta K_i / r}{A_{\text{eff},i}},
}\end{equation} where \(\beta_s^-\) (with \(s = L\)) is the negative
root of the L-regime characteristic equation
\(\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \beta(\beta - 1) + \mu_L \beta - (r + \lambda) = 0\),
incorporating the regime-switching term through the effective discount
rate \(r + \lambda\); \(c_D\) is the coupon payment; and
\(A_{\text{eff},i}\) is the effective revenue coefficient from
Equation~\ref{eq-a-eff-duopoly}. The factor
\(\beta_s^- / (\beta_s^- - 1) \in (0, 1)\) reflects the option value of
waiting before defaulting, yielding a lower (optimal) default boundary
than the naive break-even condition.\footnote{The default boundary uses
  \(A_{\text{eff},i}\), which incorporates the regime-switching
  opportunity in unconditional expectation. In a fully coupled
  regime-switching default model, equity values \((E_L, E_H)\) would
  satisfy coupled ODEs with regime-contingent default boundaries
  \((X_D^L, X_D^H)\). Three features of the present model reduce this to
  a single boundary. First, the regime switch is absorbing (\(L \to H\)
  only), so \(E_H(X)\) satisfies a standard uncoupled Leland equity
  ODE---the coupling is one-way. Second, the H-regime firm has higher
  revenue (\(\mu_H > \mu_L\), full training allocation), so
  \(X_D^H \ll X_D^L\); default in \(H\) is remote. Third, because the
  H-regime default option term (proportional to
  \((X/X_D^H)^{|\beta_H^-|}\)) is negligible at the L-regime default
  boundary, replacing \(E_H\) with its perpetuity approximation
  introduces an error of less than 1\% in \(X_D\) at baseline (verified
  numerically; see Appendix A). Under this approximation, the L-regime
  equity ODE has perpetuity coefficient \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) and
  Leland's smooth-pasting yields Equation~\ref{eq-default-boundary}
  exactly. The approximation is conservative: it overstates L-regime
  continuation value (by ignoring remote H-regime default), so the true
  L-regime default boundary is weakly higher. The faith-based survival
  threshold \(\underline{\phi}\) (Equation~\ref{eq-phi-underbar}) is
  independent of \(\lambda\): it characterizes the condition
  \(\partial A_{\text{eff},i}/\partial \lambda > 0\), which depends on
  the ratio of H-regime to L-regime revenue terms but not on the level
  of \(\lambda\) itself, since \(\lambda\) appears symmetrically in the
  numerator and denominator of \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) at the threshold.}

Crucially, \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) includes the H-regime continuation value
through the \(\lambda\) term. This creates a \emph{faith-based survival}
mechanism: a firm that invests heavily in training (high \(\phi_i\))
increases the H-regime component of \(A_{\text{eff},i}\), which
\emph{lowers} the default boundary. Optimism about transformative AI,
backed by training investment, extends the firm's solvency by raising
the equity value at any demand level. As noted above, the unconditional
\(A_{\text{eff},i}\) framework introduces less than 1\% error relative
to a fully coupled regime-switching default model at baseline, because
the absorbing regime structure and remote H-regime default make the
perpetuity approximation tight (see the preceding footnote and Appendix
A for details). Conversely, a firm that allocates entirely to inference
(\(\phi_i = 0\)) receives no H-regime upside, faces a higher default
boundary, and is more vulnerable to demand downturns.

The mechanism differs from the standard result that growth options lower
default boundaries in Leland-type models. In models with exogenous
growth options, increasing option value unambiguously improves solvency.
Here, the growth option value is endogenously controlled by \(\phi\),
and raising \(\phi\) simultaneously reduces current inference
revenue---creating a genuine trade-off between solvency through future
continuation value and solvency through current cash flow. The
faith-based survival condition (Equation~\ref{eq-phi-underbar} below)
characterizes precisely when the continuation-value channel dominates
the cash-flow sacrifice.

\textbf{Proposition 2} (Endogenous default with faith-based survival;
leveraged duopoly). \emph{For a levered firm in the duopoly with fixed
capacity \((K_i, \phi_i)\) and rival \((K_j, \phi_j)\), the default
boundary \(X_D\) has the following properties:}

\emph{(i) \(X_D\) is increasing in leverage \(\ell\) and the coupon rate
\(c_d\).}

\emph{(ii) \(X_D\) is decreasing in \(\lambda\), provided the
faith-based survival condition holds: the H-regime continuation value
contributes sufficiently to \(A_{\text{eff},i}\), which requires
\(\phi_i\) to exceed a threshold \(\underline{\phi}\). In the symmetric
duopoly (\(s_i^L = s_i^H = 1/2\), equal \(K\)), the threshold has a
closed-form expression:}
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-phi-underbar}{
\underline{\phi} = \frac{R}{1 + R}, \quad R \equiv \left(\frac{r - \mu_H}{r - \mu_L}\right)^{1/\alpha}.
}\end{equation} \emph{For the baseline calibration (\(r = 0.12\),
\(\mu_H = 0.06\), \(\mu_L = 0.01\), \(\alpha = 0.40\)),
\(R = (0.06/0.11)^{2.5} \approx 0.22\) and
\(\underline{\phi} \approx 0.18\). Note that \(\underline{\phi}\) is
increasing in \(\alpha\) (with less concavity in the revenue function, a
larger training allocation is needed for the H-regime term to dominate),
decreasing in \(\mu_H - \mu_L\) (a larger regime-switch growth premium
amplifies the H-regime term, lowering the threshold), and increasing in
\(r\) (higher discounting reduces the present value of the continuation
opportunity).}

\emph{(iii) For a given capacity \(K\) and training fraction \(\phi_i\)
below the interior maximizer of \(A_{\text{eff},i}(\cdot)\), there
exists a leverage-training substitution along the iso-\(X_D\) locus: a
firm can maintain the same default boundary \(X_D\) while increasing
leverage (which raises the coupon obligation in the numerator) by
simultaneously increasing the training fraction (which raises
\(A_{\text{eff},i}\) through the H-regime term in the denominator). This
is a mechanical relationship describing the set of \((\ell, \phi)\)
pairs consistent with a given default boundary, not a statement about
optimality. It implies that, empirically, highly levered AI firms should
exhibit higher training fractions than their less levered peers---not
because leverage causes training, but because both are jointly
determined by the firm's beliefs about \(\lambda\) and its financing
capacity.}

\emph{(iv) \(X_D\) is increasing in rival capacity \(K_j\), because
higher rival capacity reduces the contest shares \(s_i^L\) and
\(s_i^H\).}

Parts (i)--(iv) hold at fixed \((K, \phi, \ell)\). If the firm were to
re-optimize capacity, training, and leverage jointly in response to a
change in \(\lambda\) or \(\ell\), additional channels would arise---for
instance, higher \(\lambda\) also raises \(\phi^*\) (Proposition 1),
which further lowers \(X_D\) through the \(A_{\text{eff}}\) denominator.
The fixed-policy comparative statics isolate the direct mechanisms.

Figure~\ref{fig-default-boundaries} illustrates the interaction between
leverage, investment triggers, and default risk. As leverage increases,
the follower's investment trigger \(X_F^*\) falls---the risk-shifting
effect dominates: debt holders bear downside risk, making equity holders
willing to invest at lower demand levels, consistent with
\citet{mauer2005real}. Meanwhile, the default boundary \(X_D\) rises as
higher coupon payments increase the demand level at which equity becomes
worthless. The shaded region between \(X_D\) and \(X_F^*\) represents
the ``operating region''---demand levels at which the firm has invested
but has not defaulted. This region narrows from both sides as leverage
increases: the trigger falls (earlier investment) while the default
boundary rises (higher break-even), compressing the firm's margin of
safety. The leader trigger \(X_P\) lies well below the follower trigger,
reflecting the preemption incentive. At high leverage (\(\ell > 0.60\)),
the operating region becomes dangerously thin, consistent with the
elevated credit risk observed for highly levered frontier AI labs. This
narrow margin of safety is precisely the scenario Amodei describes: a
firm that commits to trillion-dollar compute purchases faces bankruptcy
if revenue falls even modestly short of projections, because ``there's
no hedge on Earth'' that can bridge the gap \citep{amodei2026dwarkesh}.

\subsubsection{Equity and debt values}\label{equity-and-debt-values}

Following the structural approach of \citet{leland1994corporate}, I
define \(E(X)\) as the \emph{net present value of equity} at the moment
of investment: the going-concern value of the equity claim minus the
equity holders' initial capital contribution \((1 - \ell)I(K)\). This is
the relevant object for the investment timing decision, since the firm
invests when \(E(X) \geq 0\). The going-concern equity value (market
value of the equity claim after the investment is sunk) is
\(E(X) + (1 - \ell)I(K)\).

Above the default boundary: \[
E(X) = A_{\text{eff},i}\, X - \frac{\delta K}{r} - (1 - \ell) I(K) - \frac{c_D}{r} + \left[\frac{c_D}{r} + \frac{\delta K}{r} - A_{\text{eff},i}\, X_D \right]\left(\frac{X}{X_D}\right)^{\beta_s^-},
\] where \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) is the effective revenue coefficient from
Equation~\ref{eq-a-eff-duopoly} and \(\beta_s^- < 0\) is the negative
characteristic root. The last term is the default option: it captures
the value of limited liability, discounted by the probability-weighted
factor \((X/X_D)^{\beta_s^-}\) which approaches one as \(X \to X_D\).
The bracket \([c_D/r + \delta K/r - A_{\text{eff},i}\, X_D]\) equals the
ongoing flow deficit at the default boundary; the smooth-pasting
conditions on the going-concern equity \(E(X) + (1-\ell)I(K)\) pin the
default boundary \(X_D\) in Equation~\ref{eq-default-boundary} and are
independent of the sunk equity contribution. At \(X = X_D\),
\(E(X_D) = -(1-\ell)I(K) \leq 0\): the equity holders' sunk contribution
is lost, but limited liability prevents negative payoffs, so equity is
worth \(\max\{E(X),\, 0\}\). Without debt (\(\ell = 0\)), the expression
simplifies to the NPV of the unlevered investment:
\(E(X) = A_{\text{eff},i}\, X - \delta K / r - I(K)\), treated as a
perpetuity with no endogenous abandonment option.\footnote{An unlevered
  firm with \(\delta > 0\) could in principle shut down to avoid
  operating costs. The omission of this abandonment option is
  conservative (it slightly understates equity value) and quantitatively
  negligible because the firm invests at \(X^*\) well above any
  potential abandonment threshold.}

Debt value is: \[
D(X) = \frac{c_D}{r}\left[1 - \left(\frac{X}{X_D}\right)^{\beta_s^-}\right] + (1 - b) \left(A_{\text{eff},i}\, X_D\right) \left(\frac{X}{X_D}\right)^{\beta_s^-},
\] where \(b \in [0,1]\) is the fraction of firm value lost in
bankruptcy and \(A_{\text{eff},i}\, X_D\) is the gross revenue present
value at the default boundary (the liquidation value of the firm's
productive assets, before deducting maintenance costs, following
\citet{leland1994corporate}).

\subsubsection{Preemption equilibrium}\label{preemption-equilibrium}

I solve for the subgame perfect equilibrium of the following investment
timing game. \emph{Timing:} continuous; each firm chooses a stopping
time (investment trigger), a capacity level, and a training fraction.
\emph{Strategy spaces:} firm \(i\)'s strategy is a triple
\((X_i^*, K_i, \phi_i)\) specifying the demand threshold at which it
invests, the capacity it installs, and the fraction allocated to
training. \emph{Observability:} investment is immediately and publicly
observed (closed-loop information), so the follower conditions on the
leader's realized \((K_L, \phi_L)\). \emph{Solution concept:} I follow
the preemption construction of \citet{fudenberg1985preemption}, extended
to asymmetric capacity choice by \citet{huisman2015strategic} and
\citet{pawlina2006real}. The equilibrium is solved by backward
induction: the follower's optimal response is derived first, and the
leader's trigger is then determined by rent equalization.

The key conditions from \citet{huisman2015strategic} that underwrite the
preemption construction are: (i) the value of being the leader exceeds
the value of simultaneous investment at the preemption trigger; (ii) the
follower's optimal trigger exceeds the leader's. Both conditions are
verified numerically for all parameterizations, including those with
leverage and training allocation. The richer strategy space (capacity,
training fraction, and leverage) does not alter the sequential structure
of the game; it enriches the payoff functions while preserving the
preemption logic.

By symmetry, both firms are identical ex ante. The equilibrium features
sequential investment: one firm (the \emph{leader}) invests first at
trigger \(X_L\), and the other (the \emph{follower}) invests later at
trigger \(X_F > X_L\).

\textbf{Follower's problem.} The follower observes the leader's capacity
\(K_L\) and training fraction \(\phi_L\), and chooses its own trigger
\(X_F\), capacity \(K_F\), and training fraction \(\phi_F\) to maximize
the value of its option, taking \((K_L, \phi_L)\) as given. The
follower's problem is a real option with a modified revenue function
that accounts for duopoly sharing through the regime-specific contest
functions. The optimization is over two continuous variables
\((K_F, \phi_F)\)---or three \((K_F, \phi_F, \ell_F)\) when leverage is
endogenous---with the trigger determined by smooth pasting at each
evaluation.

\textbf{Leader's problem.} The leader must account for the follower's
future entry. Before the follower invests (\(X < X_F\)), the leader
earns monopoly revenue---contest share \(s_L = 1\) in both regimes---and
the effective revenue coefficient reflects the full monopoly value.
After the follower invests (\(X \geq X_F\)), revenue is shared according
to the regime-specific contests. The leader's value incorporates both
phases and the transition between them. As with the default boundary,
the follower's entry trigger \(X_F\) is computed using the unconditional
\(A_{\text{eff}}\) framework and is not regime-contingent: a fully
state-dependent formulation would feature separate triggers
\((X_F^L, X_F^H)\) linked by the switching intensity, but the
unconditional approach preserves the key economic forces while
maintaining tractability. The leader must also account for the fact that
the follower's entry dilutes the leader's contest share in both regimes.

\textbf{Preemption trigger.} The duopoly is solved under common beliefs:
both firms share the same \(\lambda\), and the equilibrium is symmetric.
Cross-sectional variation in investment behavior
(Section~\ref{sec-calibration}, Section~\ref{sec-valuation}) arises from
comparative statics in \(\lambda\) and other primitives across firm
archetypes, not from equilibrium interaction among firms with different
priors. In a symmetric equilibrium, both firms would like to be the
leader. The preemption trigger \(X_P\) is the demand level at which the
leader's value equals the follower's option value: \(L(X_P) = F(X_P)\).
At \(X_P\), the rent from leading is fully dissipated by the threat of
preemption.

\textbf{Proposition 3} (Preemption equilibrium with training allocation
and default risk; symmetric duopoly). \emph{In the symmetric duopoly
with leverage \(\ell\):}

\emph{(i) (Existence: Analytical; Uniqueness: Computational) A
preemption trigger \(X_P \in (0, X_F^*)\) exists such that
\(L(X_P) = F(X_P)\), where \(L(X) = E_L(X) - (1-\ell)I(K_L)\) is the
leader's NPV of entering at state \(X\) and \(F(X)\) is the follower's
option value. Existence follows from continuity and the boundary
conditions \(L(0) < F(0)\) and \(L(X_F^*) > F(X_F^*)\) (Appendix A).
Uniqueness (single crossing) is verified computationally across all
parameterizations; no case of multiple crossings was found (Appendix
B).}

\emph{(ii) (Computational regularity) The leader's training fraction
weakly exceeds the follower's: \(\phi_L^* \geq \phi_F^*\). This
regularity is verified numerically across all calibration-relevant
parameterizations; no parameter configuration yielding
\(\phi_L^* < \phi_F^*\) was found. The economic mechanism is that the
marginal cost of training (forgone L-regime inference revenue) is lower
for the leader during the monopoly phase---the leader's contest share is
1 regardless of allocation---while the marginal benefit (H-regime
positioning against the future follower) is higher due to the longer
expected monopoly duration. The follower enters directly into duopoly
competition and faces a dual penalty from increasing \(\phi_F\): both a
direct capacity reduction and a contest share loss.}

\emph{(iii) (Numerical finding) Higher \(\lambda\) (more optimistic
beliefs) increases \(\phi^*\) for both firms and lowers the preemption
trigger \(X_P\).}

\emph{(iv) (Numerical finding) Higher leverage reduces the follower's
capacity \(K_F\) but may increase or decrease the preemption trigger
\(X_P\), depending on the relative strength of the debt overhang effect
(delay) versus the limited liability effect (acceleration).}

\emph{(v) (Numerical finding) The credit spread at the preemption
trigger is increasing in \(\lambda\): firms that invest earlier due to
more optimistic beliefs face higher credit risk.}

Figure~\ref{fig-competition-effect} quantifies the preemption effect,
plotting the monopolist's optimal trigger alongside the duopoly leader's
preemption trigger as a function of volatility \(\sigma\). The leader
invests strictly earlier than the monopolist for all volatility levels,
and the gap widens with \(\sigma\): as uncertainty increases, the option
value of waiting rises for both, but the preemption incentive compresses
the leader's delay disproportionately. At baseline (\(\sigma = 0.25\)),
preemption cuts the leader's trigger roughly in half relative to the
monopolist. Competition primarily affects \emph{timing} rather than
\emph{scale}: the leader's optimal capacity is numerically identical to
the monopolist's across all volatility levels (a computational
regularity; see the discussion after Proposition 1).

\section{Quantitative Calibration}\label{sec-calibration}

This section maps the model parameters to the AI infrastructure sector
using publicly available data from company filings, industry reports,
and the scaling laws literature. The calibration is \emph{stylized}: the
goal is to discipline the model's quantitative magnitudes and illustrate
cross-sectional heterogeneity, not to structurally estimate firm-level
primitives or test the model empirically. Two caveats should be noted at
the outset. First, the Leland default framework is an analytically
tractable approximation to financial distress risk; it fits public-debt
issuers better than VC-backed labs, though the qualitative predictions
apply across financing structures (see Section~\ref{sec-discussion}). As
AI labs mature, the Leland setting is becoming more literal: OpenAI
secured a \$4B revolving credit facility in October 2024
\citep{openai2024credit}, xAI arranged \$5B+ in secured notes and term
loans, and hyperscalers issued \$121B in bonds in 2025 alone. Second,
the training fractions \(\hat{\phi}\) are inferred from incomplete data
and carry substantial uncertainty (\(\pm 0.10\)). The calibration
proceeds in three steps: I first set the demand process parameters, then
the technology and cost parameters, and finally the financial
parameters.

Table~\ref{tbl-parameters} in the Appendix summarizes the calibration
status of each parameter.

\subsection{Demand Process}\label{demand-process}

The demand process parameters govern the drift and volatility of AI
compute demand under each regime. I use regime-dependent drifts
\(\mu_L = 0.01\) and \(\mu_H = 0.06\), with common volatility
\(\sigma = 0.25\). The low-regime drift captures baseline growth in
cloud computing revenue; the high-regime drift reflects the
substantially higher growth that would characterize a world in which
transformative AI capabilities generate massive new demand, calibrated
to the upper range of AI revenue growth projections from industry
analysts. The common volatility reflects demand uncertainty observed in
quarterly revenue data for cloud providers.

The arrival rate \(\lambda = 0.10\) per year implies an expected time to
regime switch of 10 years. This baseline represents a moderate prior,
intended to approximate the marginal investor's belief rather than any
particular executive's view. It is more conservative than the beliefs
implied by \citet{amodei2024machines}
(\(\lambda \approx 0.30\)--\(0.50\)) or
\citet{hassabis2025breakthroughs} (\(\lambda \approx 0.10\)--\(0.15\)),
and the dispersion across these views is precisely what generates the
cross-sectional variation in investment behavior analyzed in
Section~\ref{sec-valuation}.

The baseline discount rate is \(r = 0.12\), the risk-adjusted WACC for a
representative frontier AI lab. As discussed in Section~\ref{sec-model}
(Footnote 12), \(r\) is the WACC and \(\mu_s\) is the risk-adjusted
growth rate (the physical drift minus an appropriate risk premium, so
that discounting expected cash flows at \(r\) yields the correct present
value); all valuation uses this reduced-form framework throughout. The
baseline exceeds typical corporate WACCs because frontier AI labs carry
substantial technology risk and are typically high-growth with elevated
cost of equity; \citet{damodaran2025wacc} industry estimates give WACCs
of 7.2\% for computer software and 10.8\% for semiconductors, and
CAPM-based estimates for private AI labs with market betas of 1.5--2.5
yield a cost of equity of approximately 10.5--15.0\%. The analytical
results and figures in Section~\ref{sec-model} use \(r = 0.12\)
throughout; these illustrate model properties at a representative WACC
and do not correspond to any specific firm archetype. The firm-specific
WACCs in Table~\ref{tbl-firms} (ranging from 0.10 to 0.18) enter the
sensitivity analysis.

\subsection{Technology Parameters}\label{technology-parameters}

The revenue elasticity \(\alpha = 0.40\) governs diminishing returns to
capacity in revenue generation, entering both the L-regime inference
contest (Equation~\ref{eq-contest-L}) and the H-regime training contest
(Equation~\ref{eq-contest-H}). Holding contest share fixed (or in the
single-firm case), doubling the relevant capacity measure increases
revenue by approximately \(2^{0.4} \approx 1.32\) times---a 32\%
increase rather than 100\%---reflecting diminishing returns to scale. In
a symmetric duopoly, a unilateral doubling also raises the contest
share, so the total revenue gain is larger (approximately 50\% at
baseline). The cost function \(I(K) = cK^\gamma\) with convexity
\(\gamma = 1.50\) captures increasing marginal costs from power
constraints, supply chain bottlenecks (particularly for advanced GPUs),
and site preparation costs; I normalize the unit cost \(c = 1.0\), so
all monetary values are interpreted relative to this normalization.

The operating cost rate \(\delta = 0.03\) represents the ongoing cost of
maintaining installed capacity---power, cooling, maintenance, and
personnel---expressed as a fraction of capacity per year, distinct from
both accounting depreciation and economic depreciation of GPU hardware.
Accounting depreciation for data center equipment typically implies a
3--5 year useful life (20--33\% per year), and power costs alone can
reach \$0.05--0.10/kWh at hyperscale. In the model, the \emph{capital}
cost of hardware is captured in \(cK^\gamma\); \(\delta\) captures only
the \emph{operating} flow cost. Economic depreciation through
technological obsolescence (e.g., H100\(\to\)B200 transitions) would
justify a higher effective \(\delta\), making the baseline conservative.
The model is relatively insensitive to this parameter:
Table~\ref{tbl-elasticities} reports elasticities of \(+2.2\) for the
trigger and \(+2.0\) for capacity, among the smallest of all parameters.
At \(\delta = 0.10\), implied beliefs shift modestly but qualitative
results are unchanged; a higher \(\delta\) strengthens the paper's
conclusions by making Dario's dilemma more asymmetric, because higher
operating costs amplify losses from overinvestment relative to the
opportunity cost of underinvestment.

The training fraction \(\hat{\phi}\) estimates reflect the approximate
share of compute allocated to model training versus inference serving.
These estimates are inherently uncertain, as firms do not publicly
disclose precise training-inference breakdowns; the values in
Table~\ref{tbl-firms} are triangulated from three types of evidence.

\emph{Executive statements.} \citet{amodei2026dwarkesh} modeled the
long-run equilibrium at ``roughly 50/50 training and inference,'' noting
that ``it's not gonna be 10\%, it's not gonna be 90\%.'' Microsoft's CFO
described Azure AI revenue as ``primarily inference and post-training
workloads.'' Jensen Huang estimated that inference workloads would
require ``easily 100 times more'' compute than anticipated, with
inference already exceeding 40\% of AI revenue by late 2025
\citep{huang2025scaling}.

\emph{Firm-specific data.} The only company-level decomposition with
primary documentation is Epoch AI's analysis of OpenAI's 2024 compute
\citep{epoch2025openai}: of approximately \$7B in total cloud compute,
roughly \$3B went to training, \$2B to research and experimentation, and
\$1.8B to inference. Combined R\&D (training plus research) was
approximately 71\%, inference approximately 26\%. The key finding is
that ``the majority of OpenAI's R\&D compute was likely allocated to
research and experimental training runs, rather than the final training
runs of released models''---the model's \(\hat{\phi}\) subsumes both.

\emph{Industry trajectory.} Deloitte's TMT Predictions 2026
\citep{deloitte2026tmt} estimates that the industry-wide split shifted
from roughly two-thirds training to parity (approximately 50/50) in
2025, and is projected to reach two-thirds inference by 2026, driven by
expanding user bases, reasoning models using 10--100 times more compute
per query, and enterprise adoption at scale.

The xAI-like compute racer allocates the most to training
(\(\hat{\phi} = 0.75\)), consistent with its strategy of dedicating
owned compute (the Colossus supercluster) to frontier model training
while outsourcing inference to cloud providers \citep{musk2026colossus}.
The Google-like hyperscaler allocates the least (\(\hat{\phi} = 0.35\)),
reflecting its need to serve inference to billions of users across
Search, Gmail, and YouTube; Google's seventh-generation Ironwood TPU is
explicitly designed for inference workloads. The Anthropic-like and
OpenAI-like archetypes fall between these extremes. These estimates
carry substantial uncertainty---plausibly \(\pm 0.10\) for each
archetype---and readers should treat them as order-of-magnitude
discipline rather than precise moments for structural identification.
The moderate elasticity of \(\phi^*\) with respect to \(\lambda\)
(\(\varepsilon \approx +0.5\) at baseline, reflecting
\(\varepsilon_{\phi^*,\lambda} = (1-\phi^*)/(1-\alpha)\)) means that
\(\phi^*\) varies slowly with \(\lambda\), so the inverse mapping
\(\lambda(\hat{\phi})\) is steep: modest uncertainty in the observed
training fraction (\(\pm 0.10\)) translates into a wide range of implied
arrival rates. Inverting \(\phi^*(\lambda) = \hat{\phi}\) for each
archetype at \(\hat{\phi} \pm 0.10\) yields implied arrival rates that
span roughly a factor of two (e.g., the xAI-like archetype implies
\(\lambda \in [0.09, 0.17]\), corresponding to expected horizons of
6--11 years, while the Google-like hyperscaler implies
\(\lambda \in [0.03, 0.05]\), or 19--32 years). Despite this width, the
cross-sectional ordering is robust: xAI-like \(>\) OpenAI-like \(>\)
Anthropic-like \(>\) Google-like in implied \(\lambda\) holds uniformly
across the entire \(\pm 0.10\) band. The cross-sectional variation
(\(\hat{\phi}\) ranging from 0.35 to 0.75) is more informative than any
individual point estimate, and the qualitative results are robust to
moderate perturbation of these values (Table~\ref{tbl-elasticities}).

An important caveat: the boundary between ``training'' and ``inference''
is blurring. Inference-time scaling---chain-of-thought reasoning,
search-augmented generation, and agentic workflows---uses inference
hardware to produce capability improvements that were previously
achievable only through training \citep{brown2024inference}. The model
treats \(\hat{\phi}\) as a fixed allocation at investment time; in
practice, firms dynamically reallocate GPUs between training and
inference on timescales of weeks (see Section~\ref{sec-discussion} for
the direction of bias from this simplification).

\subsection{Baseline Results}\label{baseline-results}

Under the baseline calibration, the single-firm model with
training-inference allocation produces an investment trigger
\(X^* \approx 0.0047\), capacity \(K^* \approx 0.0067\), and training
fraction \(\phi^* \approx 0.70\), where the effective revenue
coefficient \(A_{\text{eff}}\) (Equation~\ref{eq-a-eff}) combines
L-regime inference value and H-regime training value. The H-regime
monopolist trigger (without regime switching or training allocation) is
\(X_H^{\text{mono}} \approx 0.0163\); the single-firm trigger \(X^*\) is
lower because the regime-switching option through \(A_{\text{eff}}\)
raises the effective revenue coefficient. The duopoly equilibrium with
zero leverage produces a follower policy of \(X_F \approx 0.57\),
\(K_F \approx 1.30\), and \(\phi_F \approx 0.70\), with the leader
preempting at \(X_P \approx 0.0082\). The large difference in raw scale
between single-firm (\(K^* \approx 0.0067\)) and duopoly
(\(K_F \approx 1.30\)) quantities arises because the duopoly contest
shares reduce firm \(i\)'s effective revenue coefficient, requiring
larger capacity to justify investment; comparisons of competitive
effects should be made within the same model environment.

The model is normalized by \(c = 1\) (investment cost scale), so
absolute levels of \(K\) and \(X\) are not directly interpretable as
physical units. The quantitative content resides in \emph{ratios} and
\emph{percentages}: the preemption discount
\(X_P / X_H^{\text{mono}} \approx 0.50\), the training fraction
\(\phi^* \approx 0.70\), the value-loss asymmetry (26\% vs.~6\%), and
the credit spread levels (170--310 bps). The raw scale difference
between single-firm and duopoly environments reflects the change in the
effective revenue coefficient: in the single-firm model with regime
switching, \(A_{\text{eff}}\) is large (the H-regime prospect amplifies
revenue per unit of demand), so the firm invests at low \(X\) with small
\(K\); in the duopoly, contest shares reduce per-firm
\(A_{\text{eff}}\), requiring higher demand and larger capacity to
justify entry. This is analogous to comparing a monopolist and a Cournot
duopolist in standard IO: the equilibrium quantities differ in level,
but the economic mechanisms are the same. The demand shifter \(X\) and
capacity \(K\) are abstract state variables; only their ratios carry
economic content. To map to observable units, one would set \(c\) so
that \(I(K) = cK^\gamma\) matches observed CapEx for a given
archetype---for example, setting \(c \approx \$2.0\text{B}\) maps the
duopoly follower's investment cost \(I(K_F) = K_F^{1.5} \approx 1.48\)
to approximately \$3B, matching the Anthropic-like archetype's annual
CapEx. This rescaling affects no ratio, percentage, or spread reported
in the paper; the model is intentionally unit-free in this sense. All
cross-environment comparisons use within-environment ratios or
percentage changes, which are invariant to the cost normalization.

Strategic interaction accelerates investment: the preemption trigger
\(X_P \approx 0.0082\) is roughly half the monopolist benchmark
\(X_H^{\text{mono}} \approx 0.0163\). Both firms optimally allocate
approximately 70\% to training (\(\phi^* \approx 0.70\)), reflecting the
high value of the H-regime continuation payoff at the baseline arrival
rate \(\lambda = 0.10\); the optimal training fraction is determined by
the balance between L-regime inference value and H-regime training
value.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}\label{sensitivity-analysis}

The following comparative statics are robust across the calibration
range; Table~\ref{tbl-elasticities} in the Appendix reports elasticities
at the baseline. The investment trigger, capacity, and training fraction
are sensitive to several key parameters. Higher volatility \(\sigma\)
increases the option value of waiting, raising the investment
trigger---the standard real options result that uncertainty raises the
value of flexibility; for frontier AI labs, this means that higher
uncertainty about future demand justifies more cautious investment
timing, even as firms rush to build. A higher discount rate \(r\)
reduces the present value of future cash flows through
\(A_{\text{eff}}\), but also raises the characteristic root \(\beta_H\),
lowering the option premium ratio \(\beta_H/(\beta_H - 1)\); at
baseline, the latter effect dominates, so the net effect is to lower
both the trigger and optimal capacity; the wide variation in WACC across
AI firms (10\%--18\% in our calibration) implies substantially different
investment policies even for firms with identical beliefs about
\(\lambda\). Higher \(\lambda\) increases the weight on H-regime revenue
in \(A_{\text{eff}}\) (Equation~\ref{eq-a-eff}), raising the effective
revenue coefficient, lowering the investment trigger, and shifting the
optimal training fraction \(\phi^*\) upward as the expected payoff from
training quality increases; the net effect is that higher \(\lambda\)
leads to earlier investment and a higher share of training (while
single-firm \(K^*\) is independent of \(\lambda\) by Proposition 1, the
duopoly equilibrium capacity can differ through the contest
function)---consistent with the observation that more optimistic firms
invest more aggressively and allocate more compute to frontier model
development. Finally, lower revenue elasticity \(\alpha\) (stronger
diminishing returns) reduces optimal capacity and raises the trigger;
this parameter is crucial for the contest function specification, as
lower \(\alpha\) reduces the benefit of capacity dominance in both
regimes, weakening the preemption incentive.

numerical, computed under the stylized calibration of
Section~\ref{sec-calibration}; they illustrate the model's economic
mechanisms rather than delivering empirically testable point estimates.

\subsection{Value Decomposition}\label{value-decomposition}

Motivated by \citet{berk1999optimal}, I decompose firm value into three
components. Unlike the standard growth-option decomposition---which
measures the present value of \emph{future} investment
opportunities---the exercise here is a comparative statics analysis: it
quantifies the capacity gap between a firm's current installed base and
the model's optimal scale.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Assets-in-place}: the value of existing installed capacity at
  current demand, incorporating both L-regime inference revenue and
  H-regime continuation value through \(A_{\text{eff}}\). \[
  V_{\text{AIP}} = A_{\text{eff}}(\phi, K_{\text{installed}}) \cdot X - \frac{\delta K_{\text{installed}}}{r}.
  \]
\item
  \textbf{Capacity gap value}: the incremental value from optimally
  choosing \((K^*, \phi^*)\) relative to the current installed level and
  allocation. \[
  V_{\text{gap}} = \text{NPV}(K^*, \phi^*) - V_{\text{AIP}},
  \] where \(\text{NPV}(K^*, \phi^*)\) is the net present value at the
  optimal capacity and training fraction. This measures how much
  additional value the firm could realize by adjusting from
  \((K_{\text{installed}}, \phi_{\text{installed}})\) to the optimal
  \((K^*, \phi^*)\)---a rebalancing gain, not a forward-looking option
  value.
\item
  \textbf{Regime switch value}: the additional value from the
  possibility of transitioning from \(L\) to \(H\), which depends on
  both the arrival rate \(\lambda\) and the training allocation
  \(\phi\).
\end{enumerate}

The capacity gap fraction \(V_{\text{gap}} / V_{\text{total}}\) measures
how far a firm is from optimal scale. For frontier AI labs, the capacity
gap accounts for the majority of firm value---typically
60--80\%---reflecting the fact that current installed capacity is small
relative to what the model prescribes.

Figure~\ref{fig-growth-decomposition} illustrates the decomposition
using the absorbing H-regime valuation as a simplified example (where
\(A_{\text{eff}}\) reduces to \(A_H \cdot (\phi K)^\alpha\)); the
general formulas above apply equally to the L-regime with regime
switching. Panel (a) decomposes total firm value into assets-in-place
(blue) and capacity gap value (orange) as a function of installed
capacity relative to the H-regime optimal level \(K_H^*\). When the firm
has little installed capacity (\(K/K_H^* \ll 1\)), most of its value
comes from the potential to reach optimal capacity, and assets-in-place
are small. As installed capacity approaches \(K_H^*\), assets-in-place
rise concavely (diminishing returns) while the capacity gap value
shrinks because the firm is already near optimal scale. Panel (b) shows
the capacity gap fraction, which declines steeply from near 100\% at
very low \(K/K_H^*\) to near zero once capacity reaches the optimal
level. For the typical frontier AI lab with
\(K/K_H^* \approx 0.1\)--\(0.3\), the capacity gap fraction exceeds
60\%, confirming that these firms are far from the model's optimal
capacity.

The large capacity gap fraction has implications for asset pricing:
frontier AI lab equities should behave more like growth stocks (high
beta, high volatility, high sensitivity to discount rates) than like
mature businesses, because the bulk of their value reflects the distance
to optimal scale rather than current operations.

\subsection{Credit Risk Analysis}\label{credit-risk-analysis}

The model produces endogenous credit risk through the interaction of
leverage, training allocation, demand uncertainty, and strategic
competition.

\subsubsection{Credit spreads}\label{credit-spreads}

For a levered firm with leverage \(\ell\) and training fraction
\(\phi\), the credit spread is: \[
\text{spread} = \frac{\text{coupon}}{D(X)} - r_f,
\] where \(D(X)\) is the market value of debt (which differs from face
value due to default risk) and \(r_f\) is the risk-free rate.\footnote{In
  the calibration, \(r_f = r - \text{equity risk premium}\). The WACC
  \(r\) combines the cost of equity and cost of debt; \(r_f\) is the
  benchmark rate against which the credit spread is measured. For the
  baseline calibration with \(r = 0.12\), I use \(r_f = 0.04\),
  consistent with recent long-term Treasury yields.} Higher leverage
increases the default boundary \(X_D\), reducing debt value and widening
spreads. The training fraction \(\phi\) enters through the faith-based
survival mechanism (Proposition 2): higher \(\phi\) raises
\(A_{\text{eff}}\), lowering \(X_D\) and compressing spreads for a given
leverage level. This creates a novel interaction between training
allocation and credit risk: firms that invest more in training may have
\emph{lower} default risk despite having less current (L-regime)
revenue, because the H-regime option value provides a valuation floor.

\subsubsection{Default probability}\label{default-probability}

The probability of default within horizon \(T\) is the first-passage
probability for GBM hitting the barrier \(X_D\): \[
P(\text{default within } T) = \Phi(-d_1) + \left(\frac{X_D}{X}\right)^{2\nu/\sigma^2} \Phi(-d_2),
\] where \(\nu = \mu_s - \sigma^2/2\),
\(d_1 = [\ln(X/X_D) + \nu T]/(\sigma\sqrt{T})\),
\(d_2 = [\ln(X/X_D) - \nu T]/(\sigma\sqrt{T})\), and \(\Phi\) is the
standard normal CDF. This is the exact barrier hitting probability for
GBM, consistent with the Leland-style first-passage default mechanism in
the model. Since \(\mu_s\) is the risk-adjusted (certainty-equivalent)
drift, this yields a risk-neutral default probability consistent with
the spread calculation; the physical-measure default probability (using
the unadjusted drift) would be lower.

Figure~\ref{fig-credit-risk} presents the credit risk implications.
Panel (a) shows credit spreads as a function of leverage, evaluated at a
fixed demand level. Spreads are concave in leverage: approximately 170
bps for very low leverage (\(\ell \approx 0.05\)), rising steeply to
about 260 bps at moderate leverage (\(\ell = 0.20\)) and reaching
approximately 310 bps at \(\ell = 0.70\). Panel (b) shows the 5-year
default probability (first-passage), which rises from approximately
0.2\% at low leverage to approximately 6\% at \(\ell = 0.70\). The steep
increase in default probability at high leverage reflects the shrinking
distance to default: as leverage increases, the default boundary \(X_D\)
rises while the firm's demand level is fixed, compressing the safety
margin.

\subsection{Dario's Dilemma}\label{darios-dilemma}

I now formalize the cost of belief mismatches---what I call
\emph{Dario's dilemma}, the fundamental tension between building
aggressively for an AI future and managing the financial risk of
overinvestment.

\subsubsection{Setup}\label{setup}

Consider a firm with true private belief \(\lambda_{\text{true}}\) that
invests according to
\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} \neq \lambda_{\text{true}}\). The mismatched
firm chooses trigger \(X^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}})\), capacity
\(K^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}})\), and training fraction
\(\phi^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}})\) based on its (possibly incorrect)
belief. This mismatch can arise from:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Agency problems}: managers invest conservatively to protect
  careers, even when the firm's founders believe in aggressive
  timelines.
\item
  \textbf{Strategic signaling}: a firm invests aggressively to signal
  confidence and deter competitors, even if its private belief is
  moderate.
\item
  \textbf{Bounded rationality}: decision-makers may not fully optimize
  given their beliefs.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Value loss}\label{value-loss}

The value loss from mismatch is: \[
\Delta V = \text{NPV}(\lambda_{\text{true}}, \lambda_{\text{true}}) - \text{NPV}(\lambda_{\text{true}}, \lambda_{\text{invest}}),
\] where \(\text{NPV}(\lambda_a, \lambda_b)\) denotes the expected
present value, evaluated at a common initial demand level \(X_0\), of
following the investment policy \((X^*, K^*, \phi^*)\) optimal for
belief \(\lambda_b\) when the true demand process is governed by
\(\lambda_a\). The timing discount \((X_0/X^*)^{\beta_H}\) adjusts for
different waiting times before the respective triggers are reached. I
evaluate investment \emph{outcomes} under the true demand process
(\(\lambda_a\)) but use the \emph{investment policy} derived from the
(possibly mistaken) belief \(\lambda_b\). The training fraction enters
the value loss through the revenue channel: a mismatched \(\phi\)
produces suboptimal revenue in both regimes. Throughout, percentage
value losses are reported as
\(\Delta V / \text{NPV}(\lambda_{\text{true}}, \lambda_{\text{true}})\),
the loss as a fraction of the first-best value.

\textbf{Numerical Finding 2} (Dario's dilemma). \emph{Across all
parameterizations tested, the value loss from conservative
underinvestment (\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} < \lambda_{\text{true}}\))
exceeds the value loss from aggressive overinvestment
(\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} > \lambda_{\text{true}}\)) for the same
magnitude of mismatch
\(|\lambda_{\text{invest}} - \lambda_{\text{true}}|\). The asymmetry
arises from the training allocation channel: a conservative firm
drastically under-allocates to training
(\(\phi^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}}) \ll \phi^*(\lambda_{\text{true}})\)),
forgoing the H-regime option value that accounts for most of the firm's
worth. At baseline parameters with \(\lambda_{\text{true}} = 0.10\), a
conservative firm investing as if \(\lambda = 0.02\) loses approximately
26\% of value, while an aggressive firm investing as if
\(\lambda = 0.20\) loses only about 6\%. For \(\ell > 0\), leverage
marginally increases the cost of overinvestment through higher coupon
obligations but does not reverse the asymmetry at this scale.}

The intuition is that the optimal training fraction
\(\phi^* \approx 0.70\) at baseline reflects the high value of H-regime
positioning. A conservative firm that under-allocates to training
(\(\phi \approx 0.14\) at \(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = 0.02\)) sacrifices
most of the H-regime option value, which is the dominant component of
\(A_{\text{eff}}\). An aggressive firm that over-allocates
(\(\phi \approx 0.97\) at \(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = 0.50\)) loses some
L-regime inference revenue but retains the H-regime upside, resulting in
a smaller net loss. This asymmetry implies that the cost of timidity
exceeds the cost of boldness: a firm that under-invests in training
foregoes the bulk of its option value, while a firm that over-invests in
training sacrifices current revenue that is small relative to the
H-regime payoff. However, the Amodei scenario---``I could buy \$1
trillion of compute that starts at the end of 2027\ldots{} there's no
hedge on Earth that could stop me from going bankrupt''
\citep{amodei2026dwarkesh}---highlights a distinct risk not fully
captured by this single-firm comparison: at much larger scale and with
substantial leverage, the absolute losses from overinvestment can be
existential even if the percentage loss is smaller.

Figure~\ref{fig-investment-dilemma} visualizes the asymmetry by plotting
the value loss \(\Delta V\) as a function of \(\lambda_{\text{invest}}\)
for a fixed \(\lambda_{\text{true}} = 0.10\). The loss is zero at
\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = \lambda_{\text{true}}\) (correct beliefs)
and increases in both directions, but the left branch (underinvestment,
\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} < \lambda_{\text{true}}\)) rises more steeply
than the right (overinvestment). A firm investing as if
\(\lambda = 0.02\) (deep pessimism) loses approximately 26\% of value,
while a firm investing as if \(\lambda = 0.50\) (extreme optimism) loses
approximately 23\%---a substantial asymmetry driven by the training
allocation channel. With leverage (\(\ell = 0.40\), dashed line), the
two curves are nearly identical, reflecting the negligible role of
default risk at the model's scale; at much larger absolute investments,
leverage-amplified losses would widen the gap on the aggressive side.
The shaded region marks the ``danger zone'' where value losses from
overinvestment exceed 10\%.

The expected-value metric \(\Delta V\) tells only half the story. The
5-year default probability under the true demand process reveals a
complementary asymmetry: at \(\ell = 0.40\), a conservative firm
(\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = 0.02\)) faces a default probability of
0.79\%---only slightly above the 0.64\% baseline---because it enters at
a higher trigger with less capacity and lower coupon. An aggressively
overinvesting firm (\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = 0.50\)) faces a default
probability of 5.04\%, nearly eight times the baseline, because it
enters at a lower trigger with larger capacity and higher coupon
obligations. The dilemma is thus genuinely two-sided: underinvestment is
costlier in expected value, but overinvestment carries substantially
higher tail risk.

\subsubsection{Implications}\label{implications}

Dario's dilemma has several implications. Within the model, the observed
aggressive investment patterns of frontier AI labs are \emph{rational}:
the cost of being too conservative (\(\Delta V \approx 26\%\) at
\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = 0.02\)) far exceeds the cost of being too
aggressive (\(\Delta V \approx 6\%\) at
\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = 0.20\)), so a risk-neutral firm should err
on the side of higher training allocation even when uncertain about
\(\lambda\). This illustrative comparison should be distinguished from a
formal revealed-beliefs methodology, which would require a richer model
(dynamic \(\phi\), heterogeneous information), proper identification,
and estimation; such an exercise is left for future work. The asymmetry
means that \emph{underinvestment} is more informative than
overinvestment---a conservative firm allocating little to training
(\(\phi \ll \phi^*\)) must hold genuinely pessimistic beliefs about
\(\lambda\), since the cost of under-training is so high. The asymmetric
loss function implies that firms should hedge against conservative
under-training more than against aggressive over-training, rationalizing
the observation that frontier AI labs consistently push the boundary of
training compute investment. At the scale of trillion-dollar
commitments, however, absolute (as opposed to percentage) losses from
overinvestment can be existential, especially with leverage---a
distinction the single-firm model does not fully capture.

The single-firm formalization isolates the belief-mismatch channel in
its cleanest form, but the motivating narrative (and the paper's duopoly
model) emphasizes competitive preemption as a key forcing function. In
the duopoly extension (Appendix E), a conservative firm faces an
additional strategic penalty: by investing later and allocating less to
training, it cedes the leader position to a well-calibrated rival,
forfeiting the monopoly-phase rents that drive the preemption
equilibrium (Proposition 3). This competitive channel amplifies the
underinvestment cost from 26\% (single-firm) to 38\% (duopoly), while
overinvestment costs rise from 6\% to 17\%---the asymmetry is preserved
and quantitatively reinforced (Table~\ref{tbl-duopoly-dilemma}).

\subsection{Equity Valuation
Sensitivity}\label{equity-valuation-sensitivity}

The model generates strong predictions about how equity valuation
depends on AI timeline beliefs. I compute option values across a range
of \(\lambda\) values, holding all other parameters at baseline values.

The results show that equity value is increasing in \(\lambda\) and
locally convex over the economically relevant range
\(\lambda \in [0.1, 0.5]\): higher \(\lambda\) implies earlier
investment with a higher training fraction, generating more value from
the growth option, and the marginal effect of higher \(\lambda\) is
itself increasing over this range, so the valuation gap between
optimistic and pessimistic firms widens as beliefs become more extreme.
As \(\lambda \to \infty\), the option value converges to the
\(\lambda\)-independent \(F_H\) from below
(Figure~\ref{fig-lambda-option-value}), so global convexity does not
hold; however, the policy-relevant range of current market disagreement
exhibits the accelerating returns that drive the asymmetric-news
prediction. This local convexity implies that a portfolio of frontier AI
labs should exhibit positive convexity to AI timeline news: good news
about AI capabilities should increase valuations more than bad news
decreases them, because the option value is convex in the underlying
arrival rate over the range that matters. The training allocation
channel amplifies this effect: at higher \(\lambda\), firms not only
invest earlier but allocate more to training, compounding the H-regime
upside.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec-discussion}

The duopoly model generates its economic content through three core
tensions: (i) current cash flow versus future capability (inference
versus training), (ii) waiting value versus preemption pressure, and
(iii) growth-option upside versus leverage and default risk. This
section discusses implications, testable predictions, and limitations.

\subsection{Welfare and
Overinvestment}\label{welfare-and-overinvestment}

The strategic preemption mechanism generates potential overinvestment
relative to the social optimum. In the duopoly equilibrium, the leader
invests earlier than the socially optimal timing to capture first-mover
advantage, and the follower invests to preserve market share. Total
industry capacity may exceed the cooperative (cartel) level, dissipating
rents.

A natural extension would endogenize the arrival rate as a function of
aggregate training compute, so that firms' training investments
collectively accelerate the regime switch. Such an extension would
generate a positive externality: each firm's training contributes to the
common arrival rate, benefiting all participants. In the decentralized
equilibrium, firms would underweight this externality, implying that the
socially optimal level of training investment may exceed the competitive
equilibrium level. This creates a potential tension: the preemption
mechanism generates \emph{over-investment} in total capacity, but the
training externality may generate \emph{under-investment} in training.

\subsection{Testable Predictions}\label{testable-predictions}

The model generates several cross-sectional predictions that are in
principle testable using the (admittedly small) cross-section of
frontier AI labs:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Training-credit spread nexus.} Firms with higher
  \(\hat{\phi}\) should have \emph{lower} credit spreads conditional on
  leverage (Proposition 2, faith-based survival): the H-regime option
  value provides a valuation floor that reduces default risk.
\item
  \textbf{Training-beta relationship.} Firms with higher \(\hat{\phi}\)
  should have higher equity betas, because a larger share of their value
  derives from the growth option (H-regime upside) rather than
  assets-in-place, making them more sensitive to systematic risk.
\item
  \textbf{Asymmetric response to AI news.} The convexity of equity value
  in \(\lambda\) (Section~\ref{sec-valuation}) implies that positive AI
  capability news should increase AI lab valuations more than negative
  news decreases them, with the effect stronger for high-\(\hat{\phi}\)
  firms.
\item
  \textbf{Leader-follower training gap.} In markets with clear
  sequential entry, first movers should allocate more to training than
  later entrants (Proposition 3(ii)), because the monopoly phase reduces
  the opportunity cost of training.
\end{enumerate}

These predictions are directional and could be assessed as the
cross-section of publicly traded AI labs expands, though the small
sample size currently limits formal statistical testing. More broadly,
the model provides a framework for interpreting empirical patterns in
the emerging AI sector through the lens of existing corporate finance
and industrial organization methods. Credit spread regressions could
test the training-spread nexus using training fraction proxies (e.g.,
ratio of pre-training compute to total compute, disclosed in some
filings). Event studies around AI capability announcements (model
releases, benchmark results) could test the predicted asymmetric equity
response. And cross-sectional investment regressions could test whether
the model's comparative statics---investment intensity increasing in
\(\lambda\) proxies, training fraction increasing in leverage
conditional on beliefs---hold in the data. These empirical exercises
require data that is currently scarce but becoming available as AI firms
go public and disclosure norms develop.

\subsection{Policy Implications}\label{policy-implications}

The model has implications for financial stability, competition policy,
and disclosure. On financial stability, the combination of irreversible
investment, high leverage, and regime-dependent demand creates potential
for systemic risk: if multiple highly levered AI labs default
simultaneously during a low-demand period, fire sales could depress
asset values and trigger contagion. The faith-based survival mechanism
(Proposition 2) suggests that optimism about AI timelines may be
propping up equity values, masking underlying credit risk.

On competition policy, the preemption mechanism implies that capacity
investment serves a dual purpose---generating revenue and deterring
rivals.

On disclosure, requiring AI labs to disclose compute expenditure
\emph{and training allocation} in standardized formats would improve
market efficiency and enable better risk assessment. The training
fraction \(\hat{\phi}\) is particularly informative because it reveals
the firm's intertemporal trade-off between current revenue and future
capability.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Direction of Bias from Static
\(\phi\)}{Direction of Bias from Static \textbackslash phi}}\label{direction-of-bias-from-static-phi}

The assumption that \(\phi\) is fixed at investment time is the
most-flagged limitation. A two-period extension provides intuition for
the direction of bias. Suppose the firm chooses \(\phi_1\) at investment
time and can update to \(\phi_2\) after observing demand for one period.

If the regime switch has not occurred after one period, the remaining
expected benefit from training has declined through discounting alone
(the H-regime payoff is one period further away in present-value terms),
even though the memoryless property of the exponential switch time means
the hazard rate \(\lambda\) is unchanged.\footnote{Under an extension
  with unknown \(\lambda\) and Bayesian learning, non-arrival would
  additionally lower the posterior arrival rate, reinforcing the shift
  toward inference. The argument here does not require
  learning---discounting alone generates the reallocation incentive.}
The optimal response is to \emph{decrease} \(\phi\)---shift from
training to inference to bolster L-regime revenue. If the regime switch
\emph{has} occurred, the firm wants to \emph{increase} \(\phi\) to
capture H-regime market share.

The option to reallocate is therefore valuable, and its availability at
the initial date affects the optimal \(\phi_1\). With the option to
increase \(\phi\) later if the switch occurs, the firm can afford to
start with \emph{lower} \(\phi_1\) (more inference) and shift to
training upon observing the switch. The static model, which lacks this
option, forces the firm to ``pre-commit'' to its long-run training
allocation, biasing \(\phi^*\) upward relative to the dynamic optimum.

This suggests that the static model \emph{overstates} the initial
training fraction: the calibrated \(\phi^* \approx 0.70\) is an upper
bound on what the static model prescribes; under dynamic reallocation,
the equivalent static \(\phi\) would be lower because part of the
training allocation is deferred to the post-switch period. Firms in
practice can and do reallocate GPUs between training and inference on
timescales of weeks. The implied \(\lambda\) that rationalizes a given
observed \(\hat{\phi}\) would therefore be \emph{higher} under the
dynamic model than under the static model, because the observed
\(\hat{\phi}\) represents a deliberate choice despite the option to
reallocate, rather than an irreversible commitment.

Dynamic reallocation would attenuate the paper's headline mechanisms but
not eliminate them. The faith-based survival channel (Proposition 2)
would be weakened because a struggling firm could shift from training to
inference to boost L-regime cash flow, partially offsetting the
default-boundary reduction from high \(\phi\). However, the channel
survives as long as the firm's expected post-switch revenue (which
depends on the \emph{cumulative} training investment, not just the
current allocation) contributes to \(A_{\text{eff}}\)---and the option
to reallocate \emph{toward} training upon a switch reinforces the
continuation-value floor. The Dario's dilemma asymmetry (Numerical
Finding 2) would be attenuated because an over-training firm could
partially recover by reallocating to inference, reducing the cost of
aggressive overinvestment. The asymmetry between underinvestment and
overinvestment would narrow but persist, because the dominant
channel---the destruction of H-regime option value from
under-training---operates through the \emph{level} of training
investment, not its adjustability. A two-period numerical illustration
(Appendix E, Table~\ref{tbl-dynamic-phi}) confirms these qualitative
predictions: the initial allocation \(\phi_1\) is close to the static
optimum \(\phi^* \approx 0.70\) across all adjustment costs, the value
gain from dynamic reallocation is modest (1.7\% under free
reallocation), and the faith-based survival threshold
\(\underline{\phi} \approx 0.18\) is unchanged. A full dynamic treatment
(modeling \(\phi_t\) as a continuous control in the HJB equation) is
left for future work.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. The model fixes the training
fraction \(\phi\) at investment time---a reasonable first approximation
for large-scale capacity decisions with multi-year planning horizons,
but limiting given the emergence of \emph{inference-time scaling}
(chain-of-thought reasoning, search-augmented generation) that blurs the
boundary between training and inference \citep{brown2024inference}. A
dynamic extension in which \(\phi\) responds to realized demand would
enrich the model; I leave this for future work.

The regime-specific revenue structure---inference in \(L\), training in
\(H\)---is deliberately extreme, chosen to generate a clean
training-inference trade-off. In practice, inference demand likely
remains large or increases post-AGI, and training contributes to pre-AGI
revenue through model improvements. A more realistic structure with
mixed revenue sources in both regimes would attenuate the quantitative
magnitude of the training allocation trade-off while preserving the
qualitative mechanisms.

The model focuses on frontier AI laboratories as the primary economic
agents: labs make the capacity investment, training allocation, and
timing decisions that drive the model's mechanisms. Some labs build
proprietary infrastructure (e.g., xAI's Colossus supercluster), while
others rely on cloud hyperscaler partnerships (e.g., Anthropic--Amazon,
OpenAI--Microsoft); in both cases, the investment decision ultimately
resides at the lab level, even when the physical infrastructure is
co-owned or leased from a third party. The Leland (1994) structural
default framework assumes publicly traded equity and continuously
tradeable debt. Several frontier AI labs (Anthropic, OpenAI) have
complex capital structures involving SAFE notes, convertible debt, and
multi-class equity---far from the Leland setting. The Leland mechanism
is best viewed as an analytically tractable approximation to the broader
financial distress risk that all AI labs face: the model's default
boundary \(X_D\) captures the demand level at which the firm can no
longer sustain operations, whether the binding constraint is a missed
coupon (for a public debt issuer), a failed funding round (for a
VC-backed lab), or a covenant violation (for a credit facility). The
qualitative predictions---that higher training investment lowers the
distress threshold through the continuation-value channel, that leverage
amplifies overinvestment losses, and that the default boundary rises
with rival capacity---apply across financing structures; the specific
Leland closed forms are the vehicle for these insights, not the literal
description of any single firm's capital structure.

The Tullock contest function is a reduced-form representation that
abstracts from product differentiation, network effects, and switching
costs. Revenue depends on \emph{relative} capacity through a zero-sum
share function: symmetric entry by a second firm splits aggregate
revenue rather than expanding it, unlike a Cournot specification where
additional industry capacity would serve new demand. Under asymmetry,
the Tullock form produces a revenue-expansion effect (see
Section~\ref{sec-duopoly}), but it does not capture the market-growing
effect of AI adoption where investment expands total addressable demand.
This biases the model toward stronger competition effects relative to a
setting where investment expands the pie. A Cournot specification would
avoid this limitation at the cost of additional complexity (see Appendix
E).

All valuation uses a reduced-form risk-adjusted framework (see the
footnote in Section~\ref{sec-model} defining \(r\) and \(\mu_s\)),
abstracting from state-dependence of risk premia across regimes. In the
credit risk analysis, the structural default probability uses the
risk-adjusted drift, yielding a risk-neutral default probability
consistent with the Leland spread calculation; physical default
probabilities would be lower. A richer treatment would allow the risk
premium to differ across regimes---plausibly higher in \(L\) (where AI
capabilities are uncertain) than in \(H\) (where transformation is
realized)---but this would require a complete equilibrium asset pricing
model and is left for future work. The regime switch to \(H\) is
absorbing: once transformative AI arrives, the model does not allow for
reversal. In practice, an ``AI winter'' scenario is a material risk that
the model abstracts from. Finally, the model treats competition among a
fixed set of domestic firms, omitting international dynamics, export
controls, and sovereign AI initiatives.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec-conclusion}

This paper develops a model of irreversible capacity investment that
captures the distinctive features of AI infrastructure competition:
demand uncertainty with regime switching, duopoly preemption, endogenous
default risk, and diminishing returns calibrated to AI scaling laws. At
the center of the model is the training-inference allocation: firms
choose how to split capacity between model training (building
capabilities for the future) and inference serving (generating revenue
today), with the optimal split driven by beliefs about the regime-switch
arrival rate \(\lambda\). The model produces analytical investment
triggers for the single firm and semi-analytical characterization of the
duopoly preemption equilibrium with training allocation and default risk
(Propositions 1--3), supplemented by numerical findings on Dario's
dilemma.

Three core tensions drive the results.

First, \emph{current cash flow versus future capability}: firms must
balance L-regime inference revenue (needed to survive and service debt)
against H-regime training quality (needed to compete in a post-adoption
world). The optimal training fraction \(\phi^*\) is increasing in the
arrival rate \(\lambda\), meaning that more optimistic firms allocate
more to training---a prediction that aligns with the cross-sectional
variation in observed training intensity across AI labs.

Second, \emph{growth-option upside versus leverage and default risk}:
the interaction between training allocation and leverage produces a
\emph{faith-based survival} mechanism. Higher training investment raises
the effective revenue coefficient \(A_{\text{eff}}\) through the
expected post-AGI continuation value, which lowers the default
boundary---but only in parameter regions where the continuation-value
channel dominates current cash-flow weakness (Proposition 2). This
creates a knife-edge for levered firms: they must invest early enough to
capture market share and maintain enough training to sustain the
H-regime option value, but not so aggressively that they risk default if
the regime switch fails to materialize.

Third, \emph{the asymmetric cost of belief mismatches}: the duopoly
preemption mechanism compresses the value of waiting, making timing
errors especially costly. Dario's dilemma reveals that this cost is
asymmetric. Conservative underinvestment is costlier in expected value
than aggressive overinvestment for the same magnitude of belief error,
because under-allocating to training forfeits the H-regime option value
that dominates firm worth. However, overinvestment carries substantially
higher tail risk: an aggressive firm faces default probabilities nearly
eight times the baseline, because it enters at a lower trigger with
larger capacity and higher coupon obligations. Within the model, this
asymmetry implies that the extreme capital commitments observed in
practice are consistent with genuinely optimistic beliefs: the cost of
overinvesting under moderate beliefs is too high for strategic posturing
or agency problems alone to explain the observed behavior, though this
conclusion rests on the model's stylized calibration and should be
treated as illustrative rather than a formal identification result.

The model necessarily abstracts from several dimensions of the full
decision space that executives navigate. It does not capture talent
constraints, safety and alignment objectives, geopolitical competition
from actors like DeepSeek \citep{deepseek2025r1}, or the ongoing debate
about whether pre-training scaling has reached its limits
\citep{hu2024scaling}. It treats the training fraction as fixed at
investment time, whereas dynamic reallocation and inference-time scaling
\citep{brown2024inference} create a richer optimization. And it does not
address the \$600 billion revenue gap between AI infrastructure spending
and current AI revenue \citep{cahn2024gap} that may discipline the
investment race through financial markets before any technological
resolution occurs. These omissions define the frontier for extending the
framework developed here.

Several directions for future research are promising. Dynamic learning
models in which firms update their beliefs about \(\lambda\) in response
to AI capability milestones---such as the shift from pre-training to
inference-time scaling, or algorithmic efficiency breakthroughs like
DeepSeek's demonstration that frontier reasoning is achievable at
dramatically lower cost---would endogenize the belief heterogeneity
across firms. Dynamic training-inference reallocation, allowing \(\phi\)
to respond to realized demand and capability milestones, would enrich
the model's predictions about the time path of compute allocation.
Incorporating financial frictions---credit constraints, dynamic leverage
adjustment, and debt renegotiation---would enrich the default mechanism.
A \emph{formal} revealed beliefs methodology that inverts the structural
model to extract implied AI timeline beliefs from observable investment
decisions would be a natural empirical companion. The illustrative
belief inversions in Section~\ref{sec-valuation} demonstrate the
concept---they show that observed investment patterns are consistent
with high-\(\lambda\) beliefs---but fall short of a structural
estimation exercise: formal identification requires additional
instruments, proper treatment of the estimation uncertainty in
calibration parameters (particularly \(\sigma\) and \(\alpha\)), and a
richer model (dynamic \(\phi\), heterogeneous information). The
fragility of such an inversion to calibration assumptions suggests that
careful econometric treatment is needed beyond the scope of this paper.

\subsubsection*{Proof of Proposition 1}\label{proof-of-proposition-1}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsubsection}{Proof of Proposition 1}

\emph{Maintained assumptions.} The proof requires Assumption 1, in
particular conditions (A2)--(A4). It applies in the parameter region
where the simplified option value \(F_L(X) = C \cdot X^{\beta_H}\) is
valid (condition A3). The firm maximizes the option value
\(F(X_0) = (V(X^*, K, \phi) - I(K))(X_0/X^*)^{\beta_H}\) by choosing
\(X^*\), \(K\), and \(\phi\) jointly.

\emph{Step 1: Optimal trigger given \((K, \phi)\).} The value-matching
and smooth-pasting conditions yield: \[
X^*(K, \phi) = \frac{\beta_H}{\beta_H - 1} \cdot \frac{\delta K/r + cK^\gamma}{A_{\text{eff}}(\phi, K)},
\] where
\(A_{\text{eff}}(\phi, K) = [(1-\phi)K]^\alpha / (r - \mu_L + \lambda) + \lambda / (r - \mu_L + \lambda) \cdot (\phi K)^\alpha \cdot A_H\)
is the effective revenue coefficient.

\emph{Step 2: NPV at the trigger.} Define
\(b(K) = cK^\gamma + \delta K / r\) (total cost). The revenue at the
trigger is
\(A_{\text{eff}} \cdot X^* = \frac{\beta_H}{\beta_H - 1} b(K)\), so the
NPV at the trigger is: \[
V - I = \frac{1}{\beta_H - 1}\, b(K).
\]

\emph{Step 3: Joint optimization over \((K, \phi)\).} Substituting the
trigger and NPV into \(F(X_0)\): \[
F(X_0) = \mathcal{C} \cdot \frac{A_{\text{eff}}(\phi, K)^{\beta_H}}{b(K)^{\beta_H - 1}} \equiv \mathcal{C} \cdot h(K, \phi),
\] where
\(\mathcal{C} = \frac{1}{\beta_H - 1}\left(\frac{(\beta_H - 1) X_0}{\beta_H}\right)^{\beta_H} > 0\)
is independent of \((K, \phi)\). The optimal \((K^*, \phi^*)\) maximizes
\(h(K, \phi) = A_{\text{eff}}^{\beta_H} / b^{\beta_H - 1}\).

\emph{Step 4: First-order condition for \(K\).} Setting
\((\partial \ln h / \partial K) = 0\):
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-foc-K}{
\frac{\beta_H}{A_{\text{eff}}} \frac{\partial A_{\text{eff}}}{\partial K} = \frac{(\beta_H - 1)(c\gamma K^{\gamma - 1} + \delta / r)}{cK^\gamma + \delta K / r},
}\end{equation} Since \(A_{\text{eff}}\) takes the form
\(g(\phi) \cdot K^\alpha\) (both in the pure H-regime and under regime
switching), the left-hand side reduces to \(\alpha \beta_H / K\), which
is independent of \(\phi\). Solving for \(K\) yields the closed-form
expression in Proposition 1, confirming that \(K^*\) depends only on the
cost and technology parameters
\((\alpha, \beta_H, \gamma, \delta, r, c)\), not on the training
fraction.

\emph{Step 5: Existence of interior \(\phi^*\) (Lemma).}

Write
\(A_{\text{eff}}(\phi) = w_L \cdot [(1-\phi)K]^\alpha + w_H \cdot (\phi K)^\alpha\),
where \(w_L = 1/(r - \mu_L + \lambda) > 0\) and
\(w_H = \lambda A_H / (r - \mu_L + \lambda) > 0\). Since \(b(K)\) is
independent of \(\phi\), maximizing \(h\) over \(\phi\) is equivalent to
maximizing \(A_{\text{eff}}(\phi)^{\beta_H}\), or equivalently
\(A_{\text{eff}}(\phi)\).

The first derivative with respect to \(\phi\) is: \[
\frac{\partial A_{\text{eff}}}{\partial \phi} = \alpha K^\alpha \bigl[-w_L (1-\phi)^{\alpha - 1} + w_H \phi^{\alpha - 1}\bigr].
\]

\emph{Boundary behavior.} Since \(\alpha \in (0,1)\), the exponent
\(\alpha - 1 < 0\), so:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  As \(\phi \to 0^+\): \(\phi^{\alpha - 1} \to +\infty\) while
  \((1-\phi)^{\alpha-1} \to 1\), so
  \(\partial A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \phi \to +\infty\). (Inada
  condition: the first unit of training has infinite marginal value.)
\item
  As \(\phi \to 1^-\): \((1-\phi)^{\alpha - 1} \to +\infty\) while
  \(\phi^{\alpha-1} \to 1\), so
  \(\partial A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \phi \to -\infty\). (Inada
  condition: the last unit of inference has infinite marginal value.)
\end{itemize}

Since \(\partial A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \phi\) is continuous on
\((0,1)\), positive near \(\phi = 0\), and negative near \(\phi = 1\),
by the intermediate value theorem there exists \(\phi^* \in (0,1)\) with
\(\partial A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \phi = 0\).

\emph{Uniqueness.} The second derivative is: \[
\frac{\partial^2 A_{\text{eff}}}{\partial \phi^2} = \alpha(\alpha - 1) K^\alpha \bigl[w_L (1-\phi)^{\alpha - 2} + w_H \phi^{\alpha - 2}\bigr] < 0
\] for all \(\phi \in (0,1)\), since \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) implies
\(\alpha(\alpha-1) < 0\) while both bracketed terms
\((1-\phi)^{\alpha - 2} > 0\) and \(\phi^{\alpha - 2} > 0\) are
positive. Strict concavity of \(A_{\text{eff}}\) in \(\phi\) guarantees
uniqueness.

\emph{Interior solution.} Setting
\(\partial A_{\text{eff}} / \partial \phi = 0\) yields: \[
\frac{w_H}{w_L} = \left(\frac{1-\phi^*}{\phi^*}\right)^{\alpha - 1},
\] which uniquely determines \(\phi^*\) as an increasing function of
\(w_H / w_L\). \(\square\)

\emph{Step 5b: Exactness of \(A_1 = 0\) under (A3).}

The general L-regime option value
\(F_L(X) = A_1 X^{\beta_L^+} + C X^{\beta_H}\)
(Equation~\ref{eq-option-L-general}) decomposes into a homogeneous
component \(A_1 X^{\beta_L^+}\), which solves the L-regime ODE with
\(\lambda = 0\) (no regime switching), and a particular component
\(C X^{\beta_H}\), driven by the forcing term \(\lambda F_H(X)\), with
coefficient \(C = -\lambda B_H / Q_L(\beta_H)\) determined by the ODE
(Equation~\ref{eq-particular-C}).

The homogeneous term represents the autonomous L-regime investment
option: the value the firm would derive from investing based on L-regime
revenue alone. In such a hypothetical no-switching world, the optimal
trigger would be
\(X_L^{\text{pure}} = [\beta_L^+ / (\beta_L^+ - 1)] \cdot [\delta K/r + I(K)] / (A_L K^\alpha)\),
where \(A_L = 1/(r - \mu_L)\) is the L-regime perpetuity coefficient.
Assumption (A3) requires
\(\Phi_L \equiv (1 - 1/\beta_L^+)/\alpha \geq 1\), which implies \[
\frac{\beta_L^+ - 1}{\alpha \beta_L^+} \leq 1 - (1 - \alpha) = \alpha \leq 1,
\] so the markup-adjusted L-regime revenue
\(A_L K^\alpha \cdot (\beta_L^+ - 1)/\beta_L^+\) falls short of what is
needed for a finite interior solution: the standalone L-regime
investment problem has no finite optimal trigger. The admissible
solution requires \(A_1 = 0\), which I establish by ruling out both
signs. If \(A_1 > 0\): since \(\beta_L^+ > \beta_H\) (the L-regime
characteristic root exceeds the H-regime root because the effective
discount rate \(r + \lambda > r\) and the drift \(\mu_L < \mu_H\) both
push the root higher), the homogeneous component \(A_1 X^{\beta_L^+}\)
dominates the particular solution \(C X^{\beta_H}\) for large \(X\). The
option value \(F_L(X) \sim A_1 X^{\beta_L^+}\) therefore eventually
exceeds any investment NPV, implying the existence of a finite L-regime
exercise boundary---contradicting Assumption (A3). If \(A_1 < 0\): then
\(F_L(X) = A_1 X^{\beta_L^+} + C X^{\beta_H} < C X^{\beta_H}\) for all
\(X > 0\), meaning the option value lies strictly below its
regime-switching component. But the firm can always replicate at least
\(C X^{\beta_H}\) by following the pure switching strategy (wait for the
H-regime arrival and invest at the H-regime trigger), so
\(F_L(X) \geq C X^{\beta_H}\) by optimality. Therefore \(A_1 = 0\).

With \(A_1 = 0\), the two boundary conditions at the investment trigger
\(X^*\) (value-matching and smooth-pasting) constitute two equations in
one unknown (\(X^*\)), since \(C\) is already determined by the ODE.
Dividing value-matching by smooth-pasting yields: \[
X^* = \frac{\beta_H}{\beta_H - 1} \cdot \frac{\delta K^*/r + I(K^*)}{A_{\text{eff}}(\phi^*, K^*)},
\] confirming Equation~\ref{eq-trigger-phi}. The system is consistent
(not overdetermined) because the two conditions are jointly the
first-order optimality conditions of the stopping problem---they pin
down the single unknown \(X^*\) given the predetermined \(C\) and the
optimal \((K^*, \phi^*)\). \(\square\)

At the baseline calibration, \(\beta_L^+ \approx 3.01\) gives
\(\Phi_L \approx 1.67 \gg 1\), so (A3) holds with wide margin. As a
numerical cross-check, solving the full two-term problem at baseline
(allowing \(A_1 \neq 0\)) confirms that the \(A_1 X^{\beta_L^+}\) term
contributes less than 0.1\% of \(F_L\) at the optimum.

\emph{Step 6: Comparative statics.} From the implicit function theorem
applied to the FOC \(\partial A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \phi = 0\), with
\(\partial^2 A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \phi^2 < 0\):

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{(\roman{enumi})}
\tightlist
\item
  \(\partial \phi^* / \partial \lambda > 0\): Higher \(\lambda\)
  increases \(w_H / w_L = \lambda A_H\). Since \(\phi^*\) is increasing
  in \(w_H / w_L\) (from the FOC), the optimal training fraction rises.
\item
  \(\partial \phi^* / \partial \mu_H > 0\): Higher \(\mu_H\) lowers
  \(r - \mu_H\), increasing \(w_H / w_L = \lambda / (r - \mu_H)\). Since
  \(\phi^*\) is increasing in \(w_H / w_L\), the optimal training
  fraction rises. Note that \(\phi^*\) is independent of \(\mu_L\): both
  \(w_L = 1/(r - \mu_L + \lambda)\) and
  \(w_H = \lambda A_H / (r - \mu_L + \lambda)\) share the denominator
  \((r - \mu_L + \lambda)\), so
  \(w_H / w_L = \lambda A_H = \lambda / (r - \mu_H)\) does not depend on
  \(\mu_L\). \(\square\)
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Proof of Proposition 2}\label{proof-of-proposition-2}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsubsection}{Proof of Proposition 2}

\emph{Maintained assumptions.} Assumption 1(A1). Capacity
\((K_i, \phi_i)\) and rival \((K_j, \phi_j)\) are fixed at their optimal
values. The default boundary follows from the Leland (1994) framework
adapted to regime switching.

\emph{One-way coupling and the single-boundary reduction.} In a general
regime-switching credit model, equity values \((E_L, E_H)\) satisfy
coupled ODEs with potentially regime-contingent default boundaries
\((X_D^L, X_D^H)\). Three features of the present model permit a
single-boundary reduction.

First, the regime switch is absorbing (\(L \to H\) only, no
\(H \to L\)), so the H-regime equity \(E_H(X)\) satisfies a standard
(uncoupled) Leland ODE with revenue coefficient \(A_H (\phi K)^\alpha\)
and its own default boundary \(X_D^H\). The L-regime equity \(E_L(X)\)
is forced by \(\lambda E_H(X)\)---a one-way coupling.

Second, the H-regime firm has higher revenue (\(\mu_H > \mu_L\), full
training allocation pays off), so \(X_D^H \ll X_D^L\): default in \(H\)
is remote. At baseline, the H-regime default boundary (if it exists) is
below 3\% of the L-regime boundary.

Third, the H-regime equity decomposes as
\(E_H(X) = [\text{perpetuity}] + D_H X^{\beta_H^-}\), where the second
term is the default option (put-like, with \(\beta_H^- < 0\)). At the
L-regime default boundary \(X_D^L \gg X_D^H\), the ratio
\((X_D^L / X_D^H)^{|\beta_H^-|}\) is large, so the default option term
\(D_H X^{\beta_H^-}\) is negligible. Replacing \(E_H\) with its
perpetuity approximation: \[
E_H(X) \approx A_H (\phi_i K_i)^\alpha \cdot s_i^H \cdot X - c_D/r - \delta K_i/r,
\] the L-regime equity ODE yields a particular solution with coefficient
\(A_{\text{eff},i}\). Applying Leland's smooth-pasting to this equity
function gives the default boundary below. The approximation error in
\(X_D\) is less than 1\% at baseline (verified numerically by comparing
the formula below to a numerical solution of the coupled system on a
fine grid). The approximation is conservative: it overstates L-regime
continuation value, so the true L-regime default boundary is weakly
higher.

In the L-regime, the going-concern equity
\(\hat{E}(X) \equiv E(X) + (1-\ell)I(K)\) satisfies an ODE analogous to
Equation~\ref{eq-hjb-L}, with the firm defaulting when
\(\hat{E}(X_D) = 0\) and \(\hat{E}'(X_D) = 0\) (smooth pasting). The
resulting default boundary is: \[
X_D = \frac{\beta_s^-}{\beta_s^- - 1} \cdot \frac{c_D/r + \delta K_i/r}{A_{\text{eff},i}},
\] where \(\beta_s^-\) is the negative characteristic root,
\(c_D = c_d \cdot \ell \cdot I(K)\) is the coupon, and all comparative
statics below hold \emph{with \((K_i, \phi_i)\) fixed at their optimal
values}.

\emph{(i) Default boundary increasing in leverage.} \(X_D\) depends on
\(\ell\) only through the numerator via
\(c_D = c_d \cdot \ell \cdot I(K)\), which is strictly increasing in
\(\ell\). Since \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) is independent of \(\ell\) (holding
capacity and training fixed), \(\partial X_D / \partial \ell > 0\).

\emph{(ii) Default boundary decreasing in \(\lambda\) (faith-based
survival).} Holding \((K_i, \phi_i, K_j, \phi_j)\) fixed, define the
L-regime revenue flow \(a \equiv [(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha \cdot s_i^L\)
and the H-regime revenue present value
\(b \equiv (\phi_i K_i)^\alpha \cdot s_i^H / (r - \mu_H)\), both
positive. The effective revenue coefficient is: \[
A_{\text{eff},i} = \frac{a + \lambda \cdot b}{r - \mu_L + \lambda}.
\] Differentiating with respect to \(\lambda\): \[
\frac{\partial A_{\text{eff},i}}{\partial \lambda} = \frac{b(r - \mu_L) - a}{(r - \mu_L + \lambda)^2}.
\] This is positive whenever \(b(r - \mu_L) > a\), i.e., when the
H-regime revenue present value scaled by the L-regime effective discount
rate exceeds the L-regime revenue flow:
\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-faith-condition}{
\frac{(\phi_i K_i)^\alpha \cdot s_i^H}{r - \mu_H} \cdot (r - \mu_L) > [(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha \cdot s_i^L.
}\end{equation} Since \(\mu_H > \mu_L\) implies
\((r - \mu_L)/(r - \mu_H) > 1\), the factor \((r - \mu_L)\) amplifies
the H-regime term, making the condition easier to satisfy. Intuitively,
the condition requires that the training fraction \(\phi_i\) is large
enough that the H-regime continuation value contributes meaningfully to
\(A_{\text{eff},i}\).

\emph{Closed-form threshold (symmetric duopoly).} In the symmetric case
with \(s_i^L = s_i^H = 1/2\) and equal capacity, the contest shares and
\(K^\alpha\) terms cancel, and the condition reduces to
\((r - \mu_L)/(r - \mu_H) \cdot \phi^\alpha > (1-\phi)^\alpha\), i.e.,
\((\phi/(1-\phi))^\alpha > (r-\mu_H)/(r-\mu_L)\). Solving for \(\phi\):
\[
\phi > \underline{\phi} = \frac{R}{1 + R}, \quad R = \left(\frac{r - \mu_H}{r - \mu_L}\right)^{1/\alpha}.
\] For the baseline calibration (\(r = 0.12\), \(\mu_H = 0.06\),
\(\mu_L = 0.01\), \(\alpha = 0.40\)):
\(R = (0.06/0.11)^{2.5} \approx 0.22\), giving
\(\underline{\phi} \approx 0.18\). To complete the proof, note that
\(X_D\) also depends on \(\lambda\) through the markup factor
\(M(\beta_s^-) \equiv \beta_s^- / (\beta_s^- - 1)\), since \(\beta_s^-\)
is the negative root of
\(\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \beta(\beta - 1) + \mu_L \beta - (r + \lambda) = 0\)
and thus depends on \(\lambda\). The full derivative decomposes into two
opposing channels: \[
\frac{\partial X_D}{\partial \lambda} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial M}{\partial \lambda} \cdot \frac{N}{A_{\text{eff},i}}}_{>0\;\text{($\beta$-channel)}} + \underbrace{M(\beta_s^-) \cdot N \cdot \frac{\partial (1/A_{\text{eff},i})}{\partial \lambda}}_{<0\;\text{($A_{\text{eff}}$-channel)}},
\] where \(N = c_D/r + \delta K_i/r > 0\). The \(\beta\)-channel is
positive because increasing \(\lambda\) raises the effective discount
rate \((r + \lambda)\), making \(\beta_s^-\) more negative and pushing
the markup \(M\) toward 1 from below (since \(\beta_s^- < 0\) implies
\(M \in (0,1)\)) (\(dM/d\lambda = 1/[(\beta_s^- - 1)^2 D] > 0\), where
\(D = \sqrt{(\mu_L - \sigma^2/2)^2 + 2\sigma^2(r+\lambda)}\)). The
\(A_{\text{eff}}\)-channel is negative under
Equation~\ref{eq-faith-condition}. At the baseline calibration, the
\(\beta\)-channel is approximately 52\% of the
\(A_{\text{eff}}\)-channel in absolute magnitude, so the net effect is
negative. The \(A_{\text{eff}}\)-channel dominates whenever
\(\mu_H - \mu_L\) is sufficiently large (the H-regime drift premium
amplifies \(\partial A_{\text{eff}}/\partial \lambda\)), which holds
across all calibration parameterizations. The result
\(\partial X_D / \partial \lambda < 0\) is verified numerically for all
\((\sigma, \mu_H, \alpha, \gamma, \lambda, \ell)\) in the calibration
ranges.

\emph{(iii) Leverage-training substitution.} Write the default boundary
as \(X_D = \Psi(\ell) / A_{\text{eff},i}(\phi_i)\), where
\(\Psi(\ell) = [\beta_s^- / (\beta_s^- - 1)] \cdot (c_d \ell I(K) / r + \delta K/r)\)
is strictly increasing in leverage (note: \(\Psi\) absorbs the markup
factor \(M\), unlike the cost term \(N\) in the
\(\lambda\)-decomposition above). From the proof of Proposition 1 (Step
5), \(A_{\text{eff},i}(\phi_i)\) is strictly concave in \(\phi_i\) with
an interior maximizer \(\phi^*\): it is increasing on \((0, \phi^*)\)
and decreasing on \((\phi^*, 1)\). For \(\phi_i < \phi^*\), a firm can
maintain a given default boundary \(X_D\) while increasing leverage
\(\ell\) (raising the numerator) by simultaneously increasing training
allocation \(\phi_i\) (raising \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) in the denominator).
For \(\phi_i > \phi^*\), the relationship reverses: further increases in
\(\phi_i\) would \emph{lower} \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) and \emph{raise} the
default boundary, so no such substitution is available. The economically
relevant range lies below \(\phi^*\) (baseline \(\phi^* \approx 0.70\)),
where the substitution applies. Note that this is a \emph{mechanical}
relationship describing the iso-\(X_D\) locus, not a statement about
optimality: higher \(\phi\) sacrifices L-regime revenue, and the net
effect on equity value is ambiguous. Leverage affects the firm through
the coupon obligation (numerator), while training allocation affects the
firm through the expected revenue (denominator); the two channels are
distinct.

\emph{(iv) Default boundary increasing in rival capacity.} Both contest
shares
\(s_i^L = [(1-\phi_i)K_i]^\alpha / \sum_j [(1-\phi_j)K_j]^\alpha\) and
\(s_i^H = (\phi_i K_i)^\alpha / \sum_j (\phi_j K_j)^\alpha\) are
decreasing in \(K_j\) (holding \(K_i\) fixed). Since
\(A_{\text{eff},i}\) is increasing in both contest shares,
\(\partial A_{\text{eff},i} / \partial K_j < 0\), and
\(\partial X_D / \partial K_j > 0\): stronger rivals raise the default
boundary. \(\square\)

\subsubsection*{Proof of Proposition 3}\label{proof-of-proposition-3}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsubsection}{Proof of Proposition 3}

\emph{Maintained assumptions.} Assumption 1(A1)--(A2). Part (i)
separates existence (analytical) from uniqueness (computational). Parts
(ii)--(v) combine analytical motivation with numerical verification as
indicated in the proposition statement.

\emph{Part (i): Existence of \(X_P\) (Analytical).} The argument follows
\citet{huisman2015strategic}, Theorem 1, applied to the enriched payoff
functions incorporating training allocation and default risk. I verify
that continuity and the required boundary conditions hold for the
enriched model.

\textbf{Definitions.} Define \(L(X)\) as the leader's NPV of entering at
demand state \(X\): \(L(X) = E_L(X) - (1 - \ell)I(K_L)\), where
\(E_L(X)\) is the going-concern equity value of the leader's installed
project (which accounts for monopoly revenue until follower entry, then
duopoly revenue, plus the H-regime continuation value). Under limited
liability, \(E_L(X_D) = 0\) at the default boundary. Define \(F(X)\) as
the follower's option value at state \(X\):
\(F(X) \propto X^{\beta_H}\), with \(F(0) = 0\).

\textbf{Continuity and monotonicity.} The leader value \(L(X)\) is
continuous in \(X\). For \(X > X_D\), \(L(X)\) is increasing: L-regime
revenue \(X \cdot [(1-\phi_L)K_L]^\alpha\) is linear in \(X\) and the
H-regime transition adds a positive option component. For
\(X \leq X_D\), the firm has defaulted and \(E_L = 0\), so
\(L(X) = -(1 - \ell)I(K_L) < 0\). The follower option value
\(F(X) \propto X^{\beta_H}\) is continuous, increasing, and convex, with
\(\beta_H > 1\).

\textbf{Boundary conditions.} At \(X = 0\):
\(L(0) = -(1 - \ell)I(K_L) < 0\) while \(F(0) = 0\), so \(F > L\). At
\(X = X_F^*\): \(L(X_F^*) > F(X_F^*)\), where \(L(X_F^*)\) is the
leader's NPV of investing at demand state \(X_F^*\) (a point evaluation,
not a continuation value from earlier entry). At \(X_F^*\), the follower
is by construction indifferent between investing and waiting, so
\(F(X_F^*)\) equals the follower's NPV of investing at \(X_F^*\). The
leader's NPV exceeds the follower's because the leader enters with
capacity \((K_L, \phi_L)\) optimized for the leader role, earning
monopoly revenue (\(s_L = 1\)) until the follower's subsequent entry,
whereas the follower at \(X_F^*\) enters into immediate duopoly
competition. This monopoly-phase advantage gives
\(L(X_F^*) > F(X_F^*)\).

By the intermediate value theorem, there exists \(X_P \in (0, X_F^*)\)
such that \(L(X_P) = F(X_P)\).

\emph{Uniqueness of \(X_P\) (Computational).} In the standard
\citet{huisman2015strategic} framework, uniqueness follows from single
crossing: the leader value \(L(X)\) crosses the follower value \(F(X)\)
exactly once on \((0, X_F^*)\). In their model, \(L(X)\) is
approximately affine in \(X\) (installed revenue minus costs) while
\(F(X) \propto X^{\beta}\) (where \(\beta > 1\) is the positive
characteristic root of the standard GBM problem) is strictly convex with
\(F(0) = 0 > L(0)\), yielding at most one crossing.

The enriched payoff environment of this paper introduces additional
features---capacity choice, training allocation, leverage with default,
and regime switching---that modify the value function shapes beyond the
standard framework. In particular: (a) the default kink at \(X_D\)
introduces a non-smooth point in \(L(X)\); (b) the training allocation
creates an additional channel through which the leader value depends on
\(X\); and (c) the regime-switching option adds power-function terms
(proportional to \(X^{\beta_H}\)) to both \(L(X)\) and \(F(X)\),
contributing positive curvature; for \(F(X)\) this reinforces convexity,
while for \(L(X)\) the net curvature depends on the interaction with the
default kink and competitive effects. Providing analytical sufficient
conditions for single crossing in this enriched environment---while
possible in principle through restrictions on parameter ranges---would
require establishing shape properties (concavity/convexity) of the
leader value function that depend on the interaction of all model
features simultaneously.

Instead, uniqueness is verified computationally: at each
parameterization, \(L(X) - F(X)\) is evaluated on a grid of 500 points
over \((X_D, X_F^*)\) and exactly one sign change is confirmed (see
Appendix B for details). No case of multiple crossings was found across
all parameterizations tested, including those with leverage and training
allocation.

\emph{Part (ii): \(\phi_L^* \geq \phi_F^*\) (Computational regularity
with analytical motivation).} The following argument identifies the
economic mechanism; the inequality itself is verified computationally.

The leader's total value comprises two phases: a \emph{monopoly phase}
(from leader entry at \(X_P\) until follower entry at \(X_F\)) and a
\emph{duopoly phase} (after follower entry). In the monopoly phase, the
leader operates with contest share \(s_L^L = 1\) in the L-regime (no
rival in inference) and \(s_L^H = 1\) in the H-regime. In the duopoly
phase, both contest shares fall below 1 as the follower competes.

The leader's marginal cost of increasing \(\phi_L\) differs across
phases:

\begin{itemize}
\item
  \emph{Monopoly phase:} The leader's L-regime revenue is
  \(X \cdot [(1-\phi_L) K_L]^\alpha\) with \(s_L^L = 1\). Increasing
  \(\phi_L\) reduces inference capacity but does not affect market
  share---the marginal revenue loss is proportional to
  \(\alpha(1-\phi_L)^{\alpha-1}\) (the \(\phi\)-dependent component,
  with common factors \(X\) and \(K_L^\alpha\) suppressed).
\item
  \emph{Duopoly phase:} After follower entry, the leader faces inference
  competition. The marginal revenue loss from increasing \(\phi_L\) now
  includes both the direct capacity reduction \emph{and} a contest share
  loss, since \(s_L^L\) depends on \((1-\phi_L)K_L\) relative to
  \((1-\phi_F)K_F\). The two channels work in the same direction, making
  the \emph{relative} cost of training higher in duopoly compared to
  monopoly.
\end{itemize}

The follower enters directly into the duopoly phase and always faces
both channels.

The marginal \emph{benefit} of training is also higher during the
monopoly phase: the H-regime contest share is \(s_L^H = 1\) during
monopoly but falls below 1 after follower entry, so the leader's
marginal return from training investment is larger when the follower is
absent. This asymmetry in marginal benefit, combined with the longer
time over which the monopoly-phase advantage compounds, drives the
leader's higher training fraction. The monopoly-phase effect dominates
because: (a) the monopoly phase occurs first, so its cash flows are
discounted less; and (b) the duration of the monopoly phase
\([X_P, X_F]\) is endogenous and substantial (typically
\(X_F / X_P \approx 40\)--\(70\) in the baseline calibration, declining
from \(\approx 70\) at zero leverage to \(\approx 44\) at
\(\ell = 0.70\); the large ratio reflects the follower's endogenously
larger capacity, which requires high demand to justify).

A formal proof would require showing that the leader's FOC for \(\phi\)
evaluated at \(\phi_F^*\) is strictly positive, accounting for the
endogenous follower response
\(\partial(K_F, \phi_F)/\partial(K_L, \phi_L)\) and the regime-switching
arrival rate channel. The interaction of these effects makes an
analytical monotone comparative-static argument intractable in the
current model. Instead, the inequality \(\phi_L^* \geq \phi_F^*\) is
verified computationally across the full parameter space used in the
calibration: in all parameterizations tested (varying \(\sigma\),
\(\mu_H\), \(\alpha\), \(\gamma\), \(\lambda\), and \(\ell\) over their
calibration ranges), \(\phi_L^* \geq \phi_F^*\) holds; no parameter
configuration yielding the reverse inequality was found. \(\square\)

\emph{Part (v): Credit spread increasing in \(\lambda\) (computational
result).} Higher \(\lambda\) lowers the preemption trigger \(X_P\) (from
part iii). The Leland credit spread is monotonically decreasing in the
ratio \(X_P / X_D\). Although higher \(\lambda\) also raises
\(A_{\text{eff}}\) and lowers \(X_D\) (faith-based survival, Proposition
2(ii)), the trigger effect dominates: \(X_P\) decreases faster than
\(X_D\) because the preemption incentive is amplified by the training
channel. The spread therefore increases. This is verified numerically
across the full parameter space. \(\square\)

\subsubsection*{Numerical Finding 2 (Dario's
dilemma)}\label{numerical-finding-2-darios-dilemma}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsubsection}{Numerical Finding 2 (Dario's
dilemma)}

Define the value function
\(W(\lambda_{\text{invest}}) = \text{NPV}(\lambda_{\text{true}}, \lambda_{\text{invest}})\):
the present value when the firm invests at trigger
\(X^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}})\) with capacity
\(K^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}})\), training fraction
\(\phi^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}})\), and (if leveraged) leverage
\(\ell^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}})\), but demand outcomes are realized
under the true process governed by \(\lambda_{\text{true}}\).

\emph{Optimality.} By construction, \(W\) is maximized at
\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = \lambda_{\text{true}}\), so
\(W'(\lambda_{\text{true}}) = 0\). The second-order loss is therefore
\(\Delta V \approx -\frac{1}{2} W''(\lambda_{\text{true}})(\lambda_{\text{invest}} - \lambda_{\text{true}})^2\),
which is symmetric to second order.

\emph{Source of asymmetry.} The asymmetry arises from odd-order terms in
the Taylor expansion, principally
\(W'''(\lambda_{\text{true}}) \neq 0\). The following heuristic argument
identifies three contributing channels. All signs are verified
numerically across the full parameter space.

\emph{Case \(\ell = 0\) (unleveraged).} The loss decomposes into a
timing channel and a \emph{training allocation channel}:

\begin{itemize}
\item
  \emph{Capacity channel.} Since \(K^*\) is independent of both \(\phi\)
  and \(\lambda\) (Proposition 1), belief mismatches do not distort
  capacity choice. The capacity channel contributes zero asymmetry.
\item
  \emph{Timing channel.} The timing factor \((X_0/X^*)^{\beta}\) is
  approximately symmetric, contributing only weakly to the asymmetry.
\item
  \emph{Training allocation channel (dominant).} A pessimistic firm sets
  \(\phi^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}}) \ll \phi^*(\lambda_{\text{true}})\),
  drastically reducing the H-regime component of \(A_{\text{eff}}\). At
  baseline, the H-regime term accounts for approximately 70\% of
  \(A_{\text{eff}}\), so under-training destroys the dominant source of
  firm value. By contrast, an optimistic firm sets
  \(\phi^*(\lambda_{\text{invest}}) > \phi^*(\lambda_{\text{true}})\),
  sacrificing L-regime inference revenue but retaining the H-regime
  upside. The asymmetry in \(W(\lambda_{\text{invest}})\) arises from
  the composition of two effects: (i) the dominance of the H-regime
  component in \(A_{\text{eff}}\) at baseline, which means
  under-training destroys more value than over-training sacrifices, and
  (ii) the nonlinear mapping \(\lambda \mapsto \phi^*(\lambda)\), which
  is concave (the elasticity \(\varepsilon_{\phi^*,\lambda} < 1\) and
  decreasing in \(\lambda\)), amplifying policy distortions on the
  pessimistic side. Together these produce \(W''' > 0\), a source of
  asymmetry novel relative to standard real options models.
\end{itemize}

Since the training allocation channel dominates,
\(W'''(\lambda_{\text{true}}) > 0\), and underinvestment
(\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} < \lambda_{\text{true}}\)) produces a larger
expected-value loss than overinvestment of equal magnitude.

\emph{Case \(\ell > 0\) (leveraged).} Leverage amplifies the asymmetry
through the default channel: overinvestment raises \(K^*\) and
over-training raises \(\phi^*\), which together push the default
boundary closer to the operating region (higher coupon from larger
investment, lower L-regime revenue from excessive training). This
introduces an additional convex loss absent in the underinvestment case.
The amplification is verified numerically. However, the tail-risk
asymmetry runs in the \emph{opposite direction}: the 5-year default
probability under overinvestment (\(\lambda_{\text{invest}} = 0.50\),
\(\ell = 0.40\)) is nearly eight times the baseline, while
underinvestment barely raises default risk, because the conservative
firm enters at a higher (safer) trigger with less capacity and lower
coupon. \(\square\)

\subsection*{B. Numerical Verification
Methods}\label{b.-numerical-verification-methods}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{B. Numerical Verification Methods}

All numerical results are computed using SciPy 1.14 optimization
routines in Python 3.13. The single-firm and duopoly optimizations use
Nelder-Mead over \((\log K, \phi) \in \mathbb{R} \times [0.01, 0.99]\),
with the trigger \(X^*\) recomputed from the smooth-pasting condition at
each function evaluation. The log-transform on \(K\) ensures positivity
without imposing artificial bounds; multiple starting points
(\(\log K_0 \in \{-2, 0, 2\}\),
\(\phi_0 \in \{0.15, 0.30, 0.50, 0.70\}\)) are used to guard against
local optima, yielding 12 deterministic starting points in total.
Convergence tolerance is \(10^{-8}\) on function values. For the
preemption trigger \(X_P\), I use Brent's root-finding method on
\(L(X) - F(X) = 0\) over \((X_D, X_F^*)\). Single crossing of leader and
follower values is verified at each parameterization by evaluating
\(L(X) - F(X)\) on a deterministic grid of 500 points and confirming
exactly one sign change. No case of multiple crossings was found across
all parameterizations tested (varying \(\sigma \in [0.10, 0.50]\),
\(\alpha \in [0.20, 0.60]\), \(\gamma \in [1.10, 2.00]\),
\(\lambda \in [0.01, 0.50]\), \(\ell \in [0, 0.70]\)). Equilibrium
uniqueness for the duopoly is assessed by running the optimization from
the same 12 deterministic starting points; all converge to the same
solution.

\textbf{Assumption (A3) boundary.} The L-regime insufficiency condition
(A3) requires \((1 - 1/\beta_L^+)/\alpha \geq 1\). At the baseline
(\(\beta_L^+ \approx 3.01\)), this yields
\(\alpha \leq 1 - 1/\beta_L^+ \approx 0.67\). The baseline
\(\alpha = 0.40\) satisfies (A3) comfortably, and the condition holds
throughout the sensitivity range \(\alpha \in [0.20, 0.60]\). For
\(\alpha > 0.67\), the full two-term solution
Equation~\ref{eq-option-L-general} must be used; qualitative patterns
are preserved.

\textbf{Assumption (A2) at archetype-specific WACCs.} The option premium
condition (A2), \(1/\gamma < (\beta_H - 1)/(\alpha\beta_H) < 1\),
depends on \(r\) through \(\beta_H\). At the baseline \(r = 0.12\), (A2)
holds comfortably (\((\beta_H - 1)/(\alpha\beta_H) \approx 0.89\)). The
hyperscaler (\(r = 0.10\)) also satisfies (A2)
(\(\approx 0.70 > 1/\gamma \approx 0.67\)). At higher WACCs, (A2) fails
via the upper bound: the platform (\(r = 0.14\)), the frontier lab
(\(r = 0.15\)), and the compute racer (\(r = 0.18\)) all yield
\((\beta_H - 1)/(\alpha\beta_H) > 1\). All archetype-specific results
use full numerical optimization over \((\log K, \phi)\) with explicit
bounds and multiple starting points, which converges to interior
solutions in all cases; (A2) is sufficient but not necessary for the
existence of an interior capacity optimum.

\subsection*{E. Robustness}\label{e.-robustness}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{E. Robustness}

I assess robustness along three dimensions:

\textbf{Parameter sensitivity.} I vary each calibration parameter by
\(\pm 25\%\) from the baseline and re-compute all results. The
qualitative findings are robust: the asymmetry of Dario's dilemma, the
large capacity gap fraction, and the faith-based survival mechanism are
preserved across all parameter perturbations.

\textbf{Alternative contest functions.} Replacing the Tullock contest
with a Cournot competition model (where firms choose quantities and
price clears the market) preserves the main qualitative results. The
faith-based survival mechanism (Proposition 2) is robust to the
competition specification: the default boundary
\(X_D = [\beta_s^-/(\beta_s^- - 1)] \cdot (c_D/r + \delta K_i/r) / A_{\text{eff},i}\)
depends on \(A_{\text{eff},i}\) regardless of how revenue shares are
determined, and \(\partial A_{\text{eff},i}/\partial \lambda > 0\) holds
under both Tullock and Cournot for \(\phi_i > \underline{\phi}\). The
preemption incentive is somewhat weaker under Cournot because capacity
differences translate less directly into revenue differences: under
Tullock, a capacity advantage maps directly to a larger revenue share
(\(s_i \propto K_i^\alpha\)), whereas under Cournot, additional capacity
lowers the equilibrium price, partially offsetting the quantity gain.
Dario's dilemma asymmetry is likewise preserved: under both
specifications, a conservative firm that under-allocates to training
forfeits the H-regime continuation value that dominates
\(A_{\text{eff}}\), producing a steeper value-loss curve on the
underinvestment side.

To quantify the role of revenue expansion, I re-solve the duopoly
equilibrium under a ``fixed-pie'' contest that replaces own capacity
with average capacity in the revenue function, so total industry revenue

\textbf{Alternative regime structure.} Using a three-regime model
(\(L \to M \to H\)) with intermediate capabilities does not
qualitatively change the results. The two-regime model captures the
essential feature: binary uncertainty about whether AI will be
transformative.
